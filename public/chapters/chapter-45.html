The Counter
Strike

"I made you a patriot."

Standing beside Birna—drenched in diamonds, glittering like a living vault—Medea looked almost severe.

Her black satin dress bore no ornament, no embroidery. Yet against the darkness of the fabric, her skin glowed like pale marble. Her lips were crimson, her posture immaculate, her presence unyielding.

She did not shine.

She endured.

And endurance carried more authority than gold ever could.

"Birna wanted the dress so badly," Medea said calmly, snapping her Damascus fan open.

The sharp sound cut through the murmurs.

"It should belong to someone who truly appreciates it. The Claudio family has always been generous to me… so it is only right that I return the favor from time to time."

Her tone was mild.

The message was not.

The Duke's daughter had taken the Princess's gown.

The court began to whisper.

"She demanded Her Highness's dress?"

"How shameless."

"Does she think borrowed jewels make her royal?"

Catherine stepped forward, smile tight enough to crack porcelain.

"My daughter is young and foolish," she said lightly. "I apologize, Your Highness. But I did send other dresses. Why choose black for a celebration?"

The implication was delicate—and poisonous.

Medea is sulking. Medea is ungrateful.

"I received my aunt's sincerity with gratitude," Medea replied smoothly. "But His Majesty is at war. The people are hungry. How could I wear diamonds while my brother fights in the mud?"

The hall fell silent.

Marchioness Aspasia stepped forward, her silk skirts whispering authority.

"Her Highness speaks rightly," Aspasia declared. "She is the King's sister. Whether she wears silk or sackcloth, her blood remains Royal. Diamonds are unnecessary for one who needs no proof."

It was a clean strike.

Medea is royal.

Birna is not.

Medea lowered her gaze, a small, restrained smile touching her lips.

"Please don't blame Birna," she said gently. "She is young. It is natural to want to shine."

The court understood.

She is a child playing dress-up.

Birna's face burned. She stood encased in wealth, yet suddenly felt naked.

I'm wearing the most expensive dress here, she thought wildly.
So why do I feel so cheap?

The Regent Speaks

Joaquin Claudio watched his daughter's humiliation with a darkened gaze.

Useless, he thought. But it doesn't matter.

The trap is already sprung.

He stepped onto the dais, tapping his crystal glass.

Ding.

Ding.

Ding.

"My Lords and Ladies," he announced grandly, "this banquet is held in honor of Her Royal Highness Medea—the one and only Princess of Valdina."

Murmurs rippled.

"The Princess has borne the weight of the realm tirelessly in His Majesty's absence," Joaquin continued, layering praise until it suffocated. "She labors so devotedly that her health itself has suffered."

Confusion spread.

She collapsed during a chapel vigil…

Is this praise—or mockery?

"Though this banquet is unworthy of her magnificence," Joaquin said warmly, "she graciously allowed us to demonstrate our devotion."

She demanded this.

She allowed this.

She indulged herself.

"Let us remember," he concluded, raising his glass, "that her presence alone is the glory of Valdina!"

The temperature in the hall dropped.

Royalist nobles stiffened.
The Queen Mother's eyes narrowed.

Two suns cannot rise in the same sky, she thought. Is he lifting Medea above Peleus?

"For the Princess!" Joaquin proclaimed.

It was bait.

He wanted resentment.

He wanted her to look like a usurper.

He glanced toward the empty throne.

Soon, he thought. Just a little longer.

Medea's Counter

"I am deeply moved."

Medea's voice cut through the tension.

She stepped forward, black silk flowing like a raven's wing.

"My uncle's care overwhelms me," she said softly. "But I fear he is too modest."

She lifted her fan, gesturing toward the tall windows overlooking the city.

"Today is the Eve of Saint Esther—the Star of Eternal Victory."

The guests turned instinctively.

Dong.

Dong.

The distant clock tower began to toll.

"My uncle did not host this banquet for me," Medea declared, her voice firm and clear. "He held it for the King. To pray that Saint Esther's light guides His Majesty and our soldiers to triumph."

Joaquin froze.

I did no such thing.

But it was too late.

Boom.

A white beam of light shot into the sky beyond the city walls.

"Fireworks!" someone gasped.

More followed—precise, controlled arcs of magic.

The lights twisted and formed symbols in the heavens.

A spear.

A shield.

A star.

"Saint Esther's Crest!" Marchioness Aspasia cried. "It's a prayer for victory!"

A wave of awe swept the hall.

This was no indulgent feast.

It was a vigil.

"For the King!" someone shouted.

"For Valdina!"

The resentment vanished—replaced by fervor.

Medea turned to Joaquin, whose face had gone pale.

"Thank you, Uncle," she said clearly, for all to hear. "For honoring my brother so magnificently."

Her smile was serene.

Her eyes were merciless.

You tried to make me a villain, her gaze said.

Instead, I made you a patriot.
