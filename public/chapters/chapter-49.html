The Private Room

"You are working too hard, Princess," Etienne laughed, though his eyes were flat and cold. "Someone has been filling your head with nonsense."

He leaned in, breath heavy with wine.

"Young people love inventing problems. Read books. Draw pictures. Leave numbers to the adults."

Medea didn't move.

"Is that your answer?"

"It is." He smiled thinly. "Everything followed procedure."

"Then bring me the ledgers by tomorrow morning. If you followed procedure, you have nothing to hide."

The smile vanished.

"Why do you want them? It's just tedious accounting."

"Because neither Prime Minister Siseo nor I approved this banquet. I'm curious how such an expense passed unnoticed."

Etienne's eyes flicked to the door.

Too many ears.

"Your Highness, this is hardly a place for such discussions. Why don't we speak privately?"

"Lead the way."

The Parlor

The door closed.

Umberto placed a fresh bottle of wine on the table and withdrew.

Etienne loosened his cravat, his face flushed. The civility dropped like a discarded mask.

"Well? Will you answer now—or should I involve the Queen Mother?"

Etienne laughed, stepping closer. His shadow swallowed her.

"Do you really think you have leverage?"

He leaned down, eyes bulging and wet.

"Who is on your side here? The Queen Mother? If I lift a finger, do you think she can protect you?"

His voice turned oily.

"The nobles despise your lineage. The servants fear losing their positions. If an accident happened to you…" He smiled. "Who would mourn?"

"Is that a threat?" Medea asked mildly.

"It's advice. Even animals know when they're walking into a trap."

Medea leaned casually against the railing.

Your threats are useless to someone who has already died.

"That remains to be seen. Madame Quiggin said something similar."

Etienne stiffened.

"Power is fragile, Minister. It flips faster than you expect. Just like it did for Quiggin."

His face darkened.

"Are you comparing me to a maid? Do you think I'm that easy to crush?"

"Am I wrong? She told me something before she died."

Etienne's breathing hitched.

"What?"

"She said the palace is full of maggots. Creatures uglier than her. So revolting they can't even find a mate. Even nature rejects them."

Silence.

Etienne froze.

The words struck deep—into the place he guarded most fiercely.

His hands trembled.

"You—"

"Is it hot in here? Your face is very red."

Etienne grabbed the wine bottle and drank straight from it, spilling liquid down his chin.

"I will remember this. Managing the palace is sacred work. I trust you'll do better."

She left.

Etienne stood shaking.

The Service Corridor

"Damn her!"

Etienne roared, kicking over a statue.

CRASH!

He snatched another bottle from Umberto.

"It's lukewarm! You idiot!"

The bottle shattered against the wall.

"I'm sorry, Master," Umberto said calmly.

Etienne drank again, wheezing.

Umberto watched silently, fingers brushing the vial in his pocket.

Instructions: Mix with alcohol. Release the toad into the market.

The drug was already working—reacting with the residue from weeks of scented candles. Inhibitions dissolving. Rage leaking through the cracks.

"He's losing control. Faster than expected."

"More wine! Get more!"

"Yes, Master."

Umberto left the door slightly ajar.

Go on, he thought.
Show them what you are.

The Garden — Oak Tree

Cesare lowered his binoculars.

"…She really did that? She didn't flinch. Not even once."

Gallo whispered.

Cesare didn't answer.

Through the window, Medea stood composed, serene—sipping wine as if she hadn't just dismantled a minister.

"She's not prey," Cesare murmured.

She's bait.

She's the blade.

"The Venus of Valdina,"

he said quietly.

Gallo swallowed.

"…Boss?"

Cesare smiled faintly.

"This is getting interesting."
