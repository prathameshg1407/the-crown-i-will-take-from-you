Chapter 269

The Poisoned
Chalice

"Some punishments require no executioner."

High Priest Paul's palm struck the table with enough force to rattle the inkwells.

"She wishes to use a sacred relic? Has she taken leave of her senses?!"

"How can the hallowed treasures of the Holy Land be appropriated for the private whims of some mere mortal?"

He conveniently neglected to mention that this "mere mortal" was the most distinguished Princess on the continent—one of only two surviving heirs of a great empire.

According to the recent petition relayed to the Central Shrine, the Princess of Valdina—on the eve of her marriage—had requested the use of the Holy Covenant Cup during the wedding ceremony.

"She claims she wishes to 'strengthen the sacred bond'—what utter pretension! If she desires such theatrical displays, let her perform them in her own country. How dare she presume to soil our temple with such frivolity!"

Paul's argument was a spectacular leap of logic.

The Covenant Cup ceremony—wherein bride and groom shared holy water from twin chalices—was an entirely legitimate tradition, one the temple had honored for centuries.

He simply refused to grant anything to the Princess of Valdina, who remained a persistent thorn in his side.

"Surely you all agree with me?"

Paul cast about for allies among the assembled clergy.

"Indeed. After disrupting the temple once already, that Princess must believe herself above all consequence."

"Your Holiness, if you continue granting such outlandish requests, where will it end? There must be limits to favoritism."

The High Priests were unanimous in their observation: Pope Benedict had shown the Princess of Valdina a degree of generosity he had never extended to anyone else.

"When the Saintess herself first descended upon us, she was granted no such privileges. Does this not suggest the Princess is being elevated above our own holy vessel?"

The clergy murmured their fierce agreement.

Yet paradoxically, their complaints only served to highlight how starkly the Pope's treatment of Medea contrasted with his reception of Rachel.

It was as though he had trampled upon Rachel's dignity—telling her, in effect, that she must accept second place.

Rachel barely managed to force the corners of her lips upward, silently cursing the Pope in her heart.

'The senile old fox is so besotted he cannot even recognize the true Saintess standing before him.'

The Pope's inexplicable fondness for Medea likely stemmed from some instinctive affinity—perhaps drawn to the very power that should rightfully belong to his chosen representative.

"Your Holiness, we cannot permit that woman to act with such impunity. We must return to His Majesty at once and—"

"No, Your Holiness. Let the Princess have whatever she desires."

Rachel raised her brows and smiled with practiced pity.

"If we create further discord, it will only reflect poorly upon our temple. If such a small gesture helps guide the Princess toward righteous devotion, would that not please the Goddess we serve?"

"Saintess..."

Paul's expression melted into reverent admiration at the Saintess's selfless magnanimity—thinking only of the Goddess even when slighted.

With that, the High Priests departed, their footsteps echoing through the corridors until silence reclaimed the temple.

That Night

The hour had grown late, and the temple's corridors lay empty and silent.

Creak.

"Saintess? What brings you here at this hour?"

"Step aside."

"I've found it. The Covenant Cup."

Twin goblets gleamed before her—one of gold, one of silver—each etched with undulating ocean patterns.

Rachel reached for the silver chalice.

The interior was pristine, clear as a mirror's surface.

She withdrew a small vial from her robes and let its contents drip into the silver cup.

"This poison is distilled from the primordial darkness itself. No antidote known to man can counter it. For a silent, traceless death, nothing is more perfect."

The colorless, odorless toxin would seep through the bloodstream, gradually calcifying the victim's heart.

"Within half a day, the target falls into a deathlike stupor. Within a full day, all breath ceases."

She was just setting down the poison-coated chalice when—

"AAAH—!"

A searing agony ripped through her right hand.

Rachel nearly dropped the silver cup, barely managing to set it down as her arm began to tremble uncontrollably.

Even then, the sensation persisted—as though her flesh were being consumed by invisible flames. The tips of her fingers had turned black.

'What—?!'

Rachel's beautiful eyes widened in horror.

She had brainwashed the guard at the entrance, yes—but she could not afford to create a commotion and rouse the entire temple.

"Mmph—!!"

Blood welled from her bitten lip as she fought to suppress the scream clawing at her throat.

The black stain that had begun at her fingertip spread with terrifying speed, consuming her entire hand.

"Ngh—! Wha—what is this?!"

The dark tide surged onward, swallowing even the sigil of dark magic branded upon her palm.

An indescribable stench—rot and corruption made manifest—emanated from wherever the blackness touched.

Rachel's bruised skin began to split open.

Her hand was rotting away.

"Agh—! Ahhh—!"

Rachel collapsed onto the floor, her body convulsing with agony.

She had no thought to spare for her cream-colored robes, now soiled against the cold stone.

Crimson tears streamed from her bloodshot eyes.

"Those who violate the golden laws of this world can never know what retribution awaits them."

This was the beginning of the punishment that the Golden Rule had decreed.

The Princess's Palace, Valdina

A gown of purest white was carefully lifted from its velvet case and displayed in full splendor.

"Oh my..."

Everyone present in the Princess's chambers was rendered utterly speechless.

"I believe this is the most exquisite dress I have ever beheld. Madame Deboire, you are truly a master of your craft."

"Naturally. You don't imagine my reputation was built on nothing, do you?"

Madame Deboire sniffed with haughty satisfaction.

Though she hailed from a minor kingdom, her renown as one of the continent's premier couturiers was uncontested.

Medea knew her well.

'In my previous life, she refused to design Rachel's gown.'

Jason had destroyed Deboire's entire enterprise in retaliation, and she had vanished from public life entirely.

What remained beyond dispute was that, despite her legendary temperament, each new creation from Madame Deboire's atelier transformed the fashion of the continent.

And in this life, Cesare had sent her to Medea.

As the designer of her wedding gown.

"Madame, thank you for all your efforts."

Madame Deboire flushed at the Princess's quiet gratitude.

"...I doubt I shall ever pour such devotion into a single garment again in my lifetime."

This wedding dress was a masterwork born from the convergence of three forces: the First Prince's blade-sharp demands, the fortune in gold he had provided, and her own fascination with the Princess at the heart of the oracle.

It was rare indeed for the trinity of fear, fortune, and inspiration to align so perfectly.

"..."

Medea gazed in silence at the masterpiece Madame Deboire had wrought.

For the first time, the phrase "pure white wedding gown" felt tangible. Real.

"Does it... displease you?"

Anxiety crept into Madame Deboire's voice when the Princess failed to respond.

'Have I grown too intoxicated by my own vision?'

"No. It's beautiful."

Medea shook her head.

In truth, even had the gown been less magnificent—less resplendent and majestic—Medea would have been satisfied enough.

Perhaps it was because she had been scorched by too fierce a flame in her previous existence.

Neither the wedding nor the wedding dress held particular meaning for her.

For a woman who had fled from Jason without ever receiving a proper engagement, a bridal gown was nothing more than a hollow luxury—something she could never truly possess.

"Hmph! The design may seem excessive at first glance, but every stitch carries meaning."

Madame Deboire spoke urgently, concerned by her client's muted response.

"The laurel at the hem symbolizes the legitimacy of your lineage. The roses upon the sleeves represent unchanging devotion. And the golden sun emblazoned across the bodice proclaims that His Highness is your one true and rightful companion."

She indicated the gold embroidery that caught the light with brilliant radiance.

"I dare say I am the only artisan on this continent capable of elevating His Highness the First Prince's... exacting specifications into art."

Medea paused, then asked:

"His Highness—that is, the First Prince—gave you specific instructions?"

"Indeed he did!"

Madame Deboire grumbled despite herself.

Did anyone imagine it was simple to weave all those symbols into a single garment without sacrificing an ounce of majesty?

'How does one make a declaration of love so impossibly complicated when a single word would suffice?!'

"I never dreamed the First Prince would prove so... particular. Had I known, I would have fled before accepting the commission. Ah—forgive me. I sometimes speak nonsense when overworked."

Neril and Madame Pinatelli, the Chief Lady of the Household, exchanged glances as they watched Deboire hastily clap a hand over her mouth.

It was rare enough for a groom to concern himself with his bride's wedding attire in such meticulous detail.

But had there ever been a precedent for a member of royalty to display such unabashed, direct affection?

Medea found herself laughing softly, bewildered despite herself.

Even when she had borne Jason two children, she had never been acknowledged as his rightful consort.

Yet Cesare was determined to elevate her standing before the wedding had even taken place.

He seemed utterly unconcerned with how it might make him appear.

His Promise

"I will make you the most envied bride in all the world."

It was as though she could hear that confident voice once more.

"Ahaha..."

A soft, genuine laugh escaped her lips.

Neril's eyes widened.

Her mistress was smiling—truly smiling—with warmth that reached her eyes.

"Well then, I shall take my leave."

Only now did Madame Deboire feel a measure of relief, having judged Medea's response satisfactory at last.

"Once the final fittings are complete, you shall be even more radiant than you are now."

She paused at the threshold, certain she had glimpsed a familiar silhouette in the corridor beyond.

'That figure... was that not the Count of Kensington...?'

Madame Deboire had traveled extensively at the behest of the Katzen royal family. She knew the Count of Kensington—one of Perdiccas II's most trusted confidants—quite well.

"Is something the matter?"

"No. I must have been mistaken. It couldn't possibly be."

Surely the Emperor's former aide—a man who had met an unfortunate end—could not be here in distant Valdina.

Madame Deboire shook her head and departed.

Shortly after she had gone—

"My lady."

Kensington approached Medea.

"That was a near thing."

"Indeed. Dressmakers possess exceptionally keen eyes for detail."

Medea gazed after the retreating figure of Madame Deboire.

"Kensington. As I have said, you need not accompany me. If you enter Katzen, that seamstress may not be the only one who recognizes you."

Kensington was a man who had died in Katzen.

For someone who had barely escaped the Emperor's grasp, returning there was tantamount to walking into a tiger's den.

"The Red Fox network still operates within Katzen, does it not? You can coordinate through them. Your safety must take precedence."

"With respect, my lady—your safety is what takes precedence."

Kensington smiled.

"Katzen is the land where I was born and raised. I know its secrets better than anyone on this continent. Would the heavens truly forbid me from assisting in Your Highness's grand endeavor?"

"..."

If Kensington—with his intimate knowledge of Katzen's royal family and nobility—stood at her side, Medea's path would be considerably easier.

'But that would place Kensington in far greater danger.'

Kensington smiled at Medea's expression, which had grown even more carefully neutral.

Unlike his former master, his current liege genuinely concerned herself with his wellbeing.

"Please, do not worry. I am not so careless as to be easily caught—and even if I am, there are always avenues of escape."

How many assassinations and silencings had he orchestrated while leading the Red Foxes?

Katzen, once his homeland, had been no exception.

"Your Highness has declared that you will make the First Prince supreme ruler of Katzen. I wish to witness that moment at my lady's side."

Or, to be more honest—he wished to stand close enough to witness the moment of downfall, when his former master's ambitions crumbled to dust.

A desire too faint to be called revenge. Too formless to be called longing.

It had burned within him ever since his old master's betrayal.

Always.

The Holy Land of Sacramontis

The wedding day of Valdina and Katzen—the union that had shaken the entire continent—had dawned at last.
