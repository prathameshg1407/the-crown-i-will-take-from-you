The
Refusal

"When the spider refuses the fly."

The Royal Prison

"You look at me with such contempt,"

Medea said softly.

She turned her back on Etienne.

Her heels clicked against the stone floor—slow, deliberate, final.

"Your Highness! Wait!"

Etienne screamed, slamming into the bars.

"Please! Come back! I will give you everything!"

The echoes swallowed his voice.

She did not look back.

Outside the Prison

Saya hurried to keep pace beside Medea, her brows knit in confusion.

"Your Highness… why didn't you take the slip?"

she whispered.

"Everyone knows the Minister has a mountain of gold. With that money, the Duke couldn't touch you. Why leave it behind?"

Medea stopped.

She looked back at the prison towers looming behind them.

"It was bait, Saya."

"Bait…?"

"Etienne is desperate,"

Medea replied calmly.

"But desperation doesn't erase instinct. He wanted to chain me to him."

She adjusted her gloves.

"If I take his money, I become his accomplice."

"If he falls, I fall with him."

"The Regent would be the first to accuse me of embezzlement."

Saya's eyes widened.

"So even now—"

"—he was trying to buy insurance,"

Medea finished.

"Money he can't use."

"Secrets he can't sell."

"Hope he can't afford."

She smiled faintly.

"Now he will sit in the dark and wonder why none of it matters."

She turned away.

"Come. My uncle should be arriving soon—with my bribe."

The Regent's Carriage

Claudio stared at the paper in his hand.

A bearer certificate.

Seven thousand gold coins—

thirty chests' worth, compressed into a single slip.

Three days of groveling.

Liquidating estates.

Threatening merchants.

Selling favors that could never be reclaimed.

"She refused physical gold,"

Claudio muttered.

"Said it was too obvious. Told me to bring a certificate."

Catherine adjusted her pearls, lips tight.

"She knows how to hide a crime,"

she said quietly.

"That alone should frighten you."

"She's greedy,"

Claudio snapped.

"That's all. Quiggin starved her. Poverty rotted her brain."

He shoved the certificate into his coat.

"It doesn't matter. Once she seals the pardon, Etienne walks free. This ends."

Catherine smiled thinly.

"I'll handle her if she hesitates."

The Princess's Palace

The palace felt wrong.

Servants moved quietly. No laughter. No idle chatter.

Claudio noticed—but dismissed it.

"Medea!"

he called, forcing warmth into his voice.

"Your aunt and I have come."

Medea sat at her desk, tea steaming beside her.

"You look tired, Uncle."

He laughed too loudly.

"I've worked myself to the bone for you, my dear."

He placed the certificate on her desk with reverence.

"Seven thousand gold coins."

Anonymous.

Untraceable.

Even sold land to make it whole.

Then he slid the pardon forward.

"Now sign. The Minister is deteriorating."

"Yes, Your Highness,"

Catherine urged.

"This is urgent."

Medea looked at the certificate.

Then the pardon.

She did nothing.

"Uncle,"

she asked gently,

"are you truly giving this to me?"

"Yes!"

Claudio said quickly.

"It's yours. Take it."

Medea leaned back.

And sighed.

"Oh, Uncle,"

she said softly.

"I didn't think you would actually do it."

The room went still.

"What…?"

Claudio whispered.

"I said thirty chests as a test,"

Medea continued, almost sadly.

"To see if the Regent of Valdina would really bribe the Princess to free a criminal."

She looked at him—not with anger, but disappointment.

"How could I accept a bribe?"

"How could I pardon a man accused of selling children?"

Claudio's breath hitched.

"Lies!"

he shouted.

"Siseo framed him!"

"Were the remains at his estate a lie?"

Medea asked coolly.

"The Black Eyes' testimony?"

"The violation of military law?"

Catherine rushed forward.

"Your Highness,"

she said soothingly,

"you're young. Politics isn't clean. If Etienne dies because of your stubbornness—can you bear that guilt?"

Medea stood.

"No, Aunt,"

she said quietly.

"I'm young. That's why I still know right from wrong."

She pushed the certificate back.

"Take your money."

"I will not sign."

Claudio stared at the paper.

Three days of humiliation.

Seven thousand coins.

All of it—for nothing.

She played me.

Something inside him broke.

"You—"

his face darkened.

"You little witch."

He lunged across the desk.

"Give me the seal. Now."

The Mask Shatters

When Civility Dies

The bribe refused.
The plan exposed.
And the Regent—finally—shows his true face.

The spider smiles. The web is complete.
