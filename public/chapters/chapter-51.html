The Greeting

The blond bowed again with a grin.

"The Façade greets Her Royal Highness. I am Gallo—just Gallo. Trouble follows my name."

"Welcome to Valdina, Sir Gallo," Medea said, polite and small as a coin.

The masked man behind him did not bow.

He regarded her with steady, golden eyes:

An animal that had learned not to blink.

"The Spear of Saint Esther was astonishing," Gallo prattled, hands rubbing together. "Divine timing, wouldn't you say? A miracle at our hour of need."

Acares's voice cut low and dry. "Miracles come when rulers want them to. Convenient timing makes me suspicious."

Gallo made a vain show of correcting him, but his smile had thinned.

Cesare—Acares—didn't tease for sport.

He probed.

Medea met him without flinching.

"Faith is a private thing," she said coolly. "Perhaps a mercenary who sells his sword for coin would not appreciate that."

The masked man's mouth twitched.

Cesare suppressed a laugh the way a blade suppresses sound.

He liked an opponent who could bite back.

A SCREAM

split the hall.

Everyone turned.

The Unraveling

Minister Etienne stumbled into the doorway, naked, his clothes a sodden mountain at his feet.

Red-faced, eyes wild, he looked less a minister than a ragged animal exposed to midday.

"Claudio! You treat me like a dog!" he bellowed. "You think because you know me, you own me!"

Ladies gasped and folded gloved hands over their mouths; some fainted.

An elderly noble rushed forward with a cloak to shield the scandal.

To Etienne—high and reeking of whatever he'd swallowed—the old man was a monster suffocating him.

Voices in his head snarled, and he reacted with the brute certainty of a frightened thing:

He punched the noble full in the face.

The man crumpled, blood and dignity spilling in equal measure.

"I am the Minister of the Court!" Etienne screamed, beating his chest. "I am not a maggot!"

Courtiers lunged for him; he shoved them aside like twigs.

Sound skewed.

The chandeliers became a snare of light;

the music a laughter only he could hear.

Then his gaze locked on the figure in black.

Medea.

"Madame—" he croaked, and whatever stayed of shame snapped. "You—"

He charged.

"Neril!" someone shouted.

A sword flashed free, but Neril was still two steps away.

Medea didn't flinch.

She watched him as if timing a clock.

And right on cue,

a shadow moved between them.

Cesare stepped forward with the casual menace of a man who never wasted motion.

He didn't draw a blade;

instead he reached out and took Etienne by the throat.

There was no dramatics—no cinematic wrestling—

only a single, terrifying motion:

Etienne's feet left the floor.

He dangled there, thrashing and choking, the light in his eyes flaring and dying.

The hall went utterly still.

Cesare's voice was low, cold as river-ice.

"So this is Valdina's hospitality,"

he said, and the words rolled through the room like a verdict.

"Charming."

"Boss—what are you doing? My boss doesn't do charity!" Gallo hissed, half-hysteric.

Cesare's eyes found Medea's across the sea of stunned faces.

Behind the iron mask, his expression was unreadable, but something like respect—sharp, assessing—softened the steel.

"Hold him," Cesare said to no one in particular. "Do not let him touch the floor."

Guards obeyed as if they'd been waiting for orders.

They formed a human cage beneath Etienne's dangling body and hauled him down, chest heaving, face purple with rage and humiliation.

He was still naked, still exposed, still a spectacle.

Cesare watched Etienne go slack, then folded his hands behind his back and drifted to the edge of the hall like a shark that had surfaced for a look.

"You played a dangerous game tonight,"

he told Medea—quiet, direct.

"And you played it well."
