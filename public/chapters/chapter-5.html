Interlude

Shadows
& Stone

"A curse must bite that deep."

"You know the story. The King's only sister. They say she's cursed—she killed her own parents."

Gallo leaned back, feet on the sill, enjoying the sound of his own voice.

"Cursed?"

"About ten years back. The former King and Queen died on the way to see her. The kingdom fell apart. Everyone whispered it was the Princess's doing."

Gallo let a theatrical sneer cross his handsome, scarred face.

"Valdina breeds brave knights, not subtle minds. Imagine the whole country trembling over a little girl."

"And you believe that?"

The voice from the shadows asked, low and bored.

"Well—no," Gallo shrugged. "But the timing was suspicious. People love a scapegoat."

The Darkness Stirs

"You brought a fool."

The shadow moved; sunlight recoiled from it.

Gallo's grin froze. The room chilled.

Fingers rested on the map, already paling to a cold, bluish tint.

"Your Highness… forgive me. I meant no disrespect."

Gallo had been there. He remembered the border three years ago.

—The Southern Border of the Kazen Empire—

Cesare, First Prince of Kazen, had ordered the Kikweg wiped clean. They were not merely enemies; they worshipped a thing that ate and fed on people.

Cesare sat on his charger as fires flared, silver armor flashing in the smoky sky.

"Burn them. Burn every last one."

They tied the chieftain to a pyre. He screamed, eyes rolling, and laughed with a wet, guttural defiance.

"Tell your god that the blood in your veins will run cold and blue."

"Your heart will turn to stone."

"You will watch your victories rot."

Black smoke braided into Cesare like a lash.

When he woke, his sword arm was leaden. His lungs strained. The healers whispered a word like a curse: petrification. Three years at best.

Cesare had railed, summoned the greatest physicians, threatened even the Pope. Nothing shivered the thing within him.

"A curse must bite that deep. It must be strong enough to pull a god from the sky. A little girl bringing bad luck? That's sympathy, not a curse."

— Cesare, First Prince of Kazen

Present Day

"Fair point. By the way, the Princess woke up. She beat the Head Maid—publicly. Maybe we plant a spy in her palace? Sounds fun."

"Have you found the Priestess?"

Cesare's tone thinned.

"We're looking! Last trace—Valdina. The trail goes cold at the border."

The Priestess of the Shadeya

The only ones who spoke the Primordial Darkness's language. The only hope Cesare had left.

"If she's in the palace, fine."

He didn't finish: the rest was a threat that needed no words.

"Also—palace banquet this week. Don't forget your mask."

Gallo tapped a silver half-mask on the table.

Cesare studied the mask with thin contempt. Fitting it on always felt like lowering a lion's head. As soon as it hid his face, he became something else—Fasade, a merchant of death with a ledger.

"Do you hate hiding your face?"

Gallo teased.

"You talk too much."

Cesare's voice was a knife.

Gallo slammed for the door—just in time for a silver dagger to bury itself into the wood where his mouth had been a second before.

Cesare didn't look up. He twirled a second dagger through fingers that were already unsteady with that old, slow cold.

— The Princess's Palace —

A corridor of petty tyrants and polished resentment.

"So she just… left? You watched her go?"

Maria shrieked, pacing and furious. She was the late nurse's daughter—dressed like a noble, brittle with entitlement.

"She ran off. We couldn't stop her."

Maria's voice snapped, and she slapped the nearest maid so hard it echoed.

"Wake up! Do you want to be thrown in the street?"

She grabbed a porcelain vase and raised it like a threat. When the line of maids didn't scatter, her practiced cruelty sharpened.

"Your Highness!"

A voice at the door called.

Maria spun, rehearsed concern plastered over malice.

"Your Highness! We were worried—where have you been?"

Then she stopped.

Standing in the doorway was Medea. She wasn't alone. Leaning on her, filthy and bandaged, was Neril—the maid Maria had always tried to ruin.

Medea looked at Maria. The expression on the Princess's face was not the sweet, grateful adoration Maria had been trained to expect.

It was cold—something clean and inexorable.

All the practiced cruelty on Maria's face fell away like costume.

Medea's eyes were small, pale lanterns. They held no tenderness, only a quiet question that felt like accusation.

Maria, for once, found nothing to say. Her lips tightened. The vase trembled in her hand.

"Where are they?"

Medea's voice was small, but it filled the hall.

It was not the childish curiosity of a spoiled girl. It was an order folded into a sentence—a line drawn.

Maria's smile died like a struck candle.
