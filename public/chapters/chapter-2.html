Ambient: Cellar
Chapter Two

The Silent
Loyalty

"Just one word, and I'll leave."

"Are you truly going to stay silent? I take no pleasure in this. Just one word, and I'll leave."

Crack.

The whip screamed through the air and bit deep.

— Kh…

"What? You'll only understand once you see bone? If you die here, no one will know. Not even your precious mistress. You'll rot in this cellar like a dog."

Neril shut her eyes.

Jena's breathing was uneven now—rage mixed with exertion. The silence only made her crueler.

"Good," Jena muttered. "Let's see how long loyalty lasts."

Another strike tore a broken sound from Neril's throat.

"You chose poorly," Jena sneered. "Playing knight for a little girl who won't even notice you're gone."

Mistress.

The word anchored Neril against the pain.

Is Her Highness awake?

Is she looking for me?

She had never been gentle. No flattery, no soft smiles—only blunt words and unyielding duty. Medea disliked her for it. Neril had always known.

It didn't matter.

Once, long ago, a small hand had reached out and pulled her from the abyss.

That was enough.

If I die here… will she survive this place?

The palace was a nest of vipers. Regents. Queens. Servants who smiled while sharpening knives.

"You'll die here," Jena said, lifting the whip again. "And it won't be my fault."

The whistle began—

—and stopped.

Neril's vision dimmed, then stilled.

A figure stood before her. Small. Pale.

A hallucination.

"…Your Highness?"

The figure did not fade.

"How did you—?!" Jena shrieked.

Bang.

The whip was torn from her grasp and slammed against the stone floor.

The girl before them was real.

"What,"

Medea asked softly,

"do you think you're doing?"

Her voice was quiet.

The room bent around it.

Jena froze. The sound lodged in her chest like a blade.

"Who allowed you," Medea continued, eyes cold and level, "to take my maid?"

The air thickened.

"Who allowed you to draw her blood without my consent?"

A servant behind Medea trembled.

"Take Neril," Medea said. "Now."

The maid rushed forward, untying the ropes. Neril collapsed. A sharp gasp escaped the servant when she saw the arm—twisted, swollen, wrong.

Medea looked.

And smiled.

It was not a child's smile.

"Who told you," Medea said, "that you could leave?"

Jena felt it then.

Fear.

Those green eyes were not young. They were ancient, steady, and merciless—the gaze of someone who had survived slaughter.

"Get her out," Medea ordered.

"No—wait!" Jena lunged forward. "Guards! Stop them!"

No one moved.

Then—

"Your Highness."

The crowd parted.

Lady Quijin stepped through with practiced grace. She picked up the whip and placed it neatly back into Jena's shaking hands.

"How unsightly," she said mildly. "To make a scene over discipline."

The architect revealed herself at last.

"Your Highness," Quijin continued, smiling thinly, "you should return to bed. You are frail. This is no place for you."

"Head Maid," Medea replied, unmoving, "explain why my personal guard was taken and tortured in secret."

Quijin sighed, patient, indulgent.

"She is my subordinate first, and yours second. I serve the Blood of Valdin. Maintaining order is my duty."

She leaned closer.

"Neril failed. Her punishment was justified."

Medea tilted her head.

"You serve the Blood of Valdin."

"Of course."

"There is much of it in this palace," Medea said calmly. "My grandmother. My uncle. Their kin. Their children."

One step forward.

Quijin stepped back.

"So tell me,"
Medea whispered, her voice echoing against stone,

"among so many descendants…
which one did you choose
as your master?"
