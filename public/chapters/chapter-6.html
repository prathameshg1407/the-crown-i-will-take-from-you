Interlude

Shadows
& Stone

"A curse must bite deeper than that."

"You know the story," Gallo said lightly. "The King's only sister. They say she's cursed—killed her own parents."

He leaned back, propping his boots against the windowsill, savoring the rumor like fine wine.

"Cursed?"

"Ten years ago. The former King and Queen died on their way to visit her. The King fell, the war unraveled, and the kingdom slipped into chaos. For years afterward, people whispered that the Princess was the cause."

He let a theatrical sneer curve across his scarred, handsome face.

"Valdina breeds brave knights, not clever ones. Imagine an entire country trembling because of a little girl."

"And you believe that?"

The voice from the shadows asked, low and bored.

"Well—no. But the timing was convenient. And people adore a good scapegoat."

The Darkness Stirs

"You brought a fool."

The shadow shifted. Sunlight recoiled.

Gallo's grin froze. The room seemed to cool. Fingers rested on the map—already paling to a faint, unnatural blue.

"Your Highness… forgive me. I meant no disrespect."

Gallo had been there. He remembered the border, three years ago.

—The Southern Border of the Kazen Empire—

Cesare, First Prince of Kazen, had ordered the Kikweg wiped from existence. They were not merely enemies; they worshiped something ancient and ravenous—an evil that fed on suffering.

Cesare sat astride his warhorse as the fires rose, silver armor flashing beneath a smoke-choked sky.

Prince Cesare

"Burn them. Every last one."

They bound the chieftain to the pyre. He screamed, eyes rolling, then laughed—a wet, gurgling sound of defiance.

"Tell your god that your blood will run cold and blue."

"Your heart will turn to stone."

"You will conquer the world—and watch it rot."

Black smoke coiled around Cesare like a living whip.

When he woke, his sword arm felt like lead. His lungs strained. The healers whispered a word like a death sentence: petrification. Three years, at best.

Cesare had raged, summoned the finest physicians, threatened even the Pope. Nothing stirred the thing nesting inside him.

Prince Cesare

"A curse must bite deeper than that. It must be strong enough to drag a god from the sky. A child blamed for misfortune? That isn't a curse. It's pity."

Present Day

"Fair enough. Speaking of pity—the Princess woke up. Beat the Head Maid. Publicly. Maybe we slip a spy into her palace? Sounds entertaining."

"Have you found the Priestess?"

Cesare cut in.

"We're trying! Last trace was Valdina. Trail goes cold at the border."

The Priestess of the Shadeya

Those rumored to speak the language of the Primordial Darkness. Cesare's final hope.

"If she's in the palace, good."

He didn't finish. He didn't need to.

"Oh—and the palace banquet's this week. Don't forget your mask."

Cesare stared at the silver half-mask with quiet disdain. When it covered his face, he ceased to be Cesare. He became Fasade—a merchant of death. A humiliating role for a lion.

"Do you hate hiding that much?"

Gallo teased.

"You talk too much."

Gallo dove for the door just as a silver dagger buried itself where his mouth had been a heartbeat earlier.

Cesare didn't look up. He merely twirled a second dagger through fingers already stiffening with that old, creeping cold.

— The Princess's Palace —

A corridor of petty tyrants and polished resentment.

Maria

"So she just left? You stood there and watched her go?!"

She was the late nurse's daughter—dressed like a noble, brittle with entitlement.

"She ran off. We couldn't stop her…"

Maria slapped the nearest maid so hard the sound cracked through the hall.

"Wake up! Do you want to be thrown into the street?"

She raised a porcelain vase, poised to shatter it. She was untouchable—or so she believed.

"Your Highness!"

Maria spun, her fury folding instantly into practiced concern.

"Your Highness! We were worried—where have you been?"

Then she stopped.

Princess Medea

Medea stood in the doorway. Leaning against her, filthy and bandaged, was Neril—the maid Maria had tried to erase for years.

Medea looked at her.

Not with affection.

Not with trust.

Cold. Clean. Inexorable.

Maria's cruelty slid off her face like a discarded mask. The vase trembled in her hand.

Medea's eyes—small, pale lanterns—held no warmth. Only a quiet question that felt like judgment.

Princess Medea

"Where are they?"

It wasn't childish curiosity.
It was an order.

Maria's smile died like a candle pinched between fingers.
