Chapter 222

The Fallen
Leaf

"Even still waters can drown empires."

His footsteps carried the weight of decades—heavy, deliberate—yet moved with the quiet grace of a man who had long since learned to leave no trace.

"Marquis."

Eratosthenes betrayed no surprise at finding Reniar waiting on the bridge. His pace merely slowed, becoming more leisurely as he approached the stone pier.

"You haven't left the palace yet."

"I suspected you would take this route. After decades of navigating these halls together, we both know which paths avoid... inconvenient encounters."

The Marquis smiled, though thorns glinted beneath the pleasantry.

"And yet, why would Your Excellency squander precious time on a worthless old man such as myself?"

Reniar swallowed a bitter laugh at Eratosthenes' bone-dry response.

This old monk, with his exquisite courtesy, was once the most influential scholar in the empire. His judgment alone could determine the rise and fall of nations.

Though his formal power had scattered like autumn leaves, countless talents—regardless of birth or station—still claimed discipleship under his name, drawn by reputation and an integrity that had never wavered.

The elaborate politeness of a man who had never needed to be cruel was, somehow, more unsettling than open hostility.

"I will not waste words, Eratosthenes. Why have you returned?"

"Hmm."

The Marquis grew restless beneath that implacable calm.

"You have never been one to chase faded glory. That much I know."

"I understand your concerns, Marquis. You fear this old man has developed an... unfounded desire to profit from his blood relatives, now that it is far too late."

Blood Relatives

The Marquis stiffened at the phrase, understanding its true weight.

Eratosthenes—father of the late Empress—was the nominal maternal grandfather of First Prince Cesare. Though they shared no blood, once a man ascended the throne, such distinctions ceased to matter.

They were close enough that neither hesitated to invoke the bond of kinship.

Unlike me—who am truly blood-related to him, yet can never show it openly.

"So it is now that ambition stirs? After all these years of silence, you suddenly speak in defense of the First Prince before His Majesty? Do you not think it... rather late for such devotion?"

"Rest assured, Marquis. I have no intention of competing for your grandson's affections."

The words fell like stones into still water.

A faint breath, almost a sigh, escaped the corner of Eratosthenes' mouth. Reniar's brow furrowed—it felt as though the old man were mocking his impatience.

"It was never ancestral devotion that guided your choice, was it? Had that been true, you would have remained at the side of Grand Duke Castulo—the legitimate son."

Silence.

"I understand the Marquis' reasoning perfectly. The Grand Duke cannot claim the throne. You could not risk your power—your family's entire future—on a man destined to fail."

For a heartbeat, Reniar's gaze wavered.

The strike had found its mark. Through the cracks in his iron composure, embarrassment bled through—raw and unmistakable.

Everything Eratosthenes said was true.

As the Emperor's legitimate heir, Jason had inherited every advantage—education, resources, imperial favor. Yet he had never managed to match the brilliance of the illegitimate son, Cesare.

But that grandson—born of scandal, burdened with a nature too strange to display proudly—had proven to be something else entirely.

" It matters not. Even if the blood in my veins is considered wicked—no one can deny that it is pure. "

They say monarchs are sent down from heaven.

No danger, no hardship had ever broken that boy's spirit. Not even the circumstances of his birth—whispered to be the product of an affair between the current Emperor and the former Empress—had diminished him.

Reniar would never forget the moment he first heard that arrogant declaration. The shudder that ran through him as he recognized something beyond morality, beyond convention—something destined.

If it is this child, he will reach the summit. Someday, without question.

But time passed. The child grew.

"Marquis, we were deceived! The First Prince will drench this land in blood! We must stop him before everything becomes irreversible—"

"Reniar. The time has come to choose."

The young prince's voice had been ice and fire intertwined.

"Will you remain among these rotting corpses? Or will you follow the bloody glory I intend to build?"

When all the old men who had conspired to destroy him lay poisoned at his feet—

When Reniar stood before the purple-faced corpses of his own aides—

He finally understood.

The First Prince was never meant to be bound by the shackles of secret bloodlines. And I... I can only stand beside him as a subject.

"You chose a master closer to the throne for the sake of power, Marquis. You must also bear responsibility for that choice. Do not overstep the duties of a subject."

"It was for Katzen."

Reniar's voice took on the cadence of a man defending sacred ground.

"The Grand Duke could not do what was necessary. Only the First Prince possessed the capacity to lead this empire to unrivaled prosperity. That is why I chose him."

"And you claim there was never any intention to use him to protect your own power? Your family's bloodline?"

Those dry, knowing eyes said everything words could not.

We both know that is a lie.

Reniar fell silent, stripped bare by the contradiction.

To mask his discomfort, his fingers found the eagle pattern on his ring, hidden beneath his sleeve. The familiar ridges offered no comfort.

The shadow of the First Prince overlaps with this old man. Eratosthenes—who shares not a drop of blood with him—resembles the Prince far more than I ever could.

A bitter, fatal sense of defeat rose in Reniar's throat. He swallowed it in silence.

Eratosthenes observed all of this with an expression of subtle fatigue, tinged with ancient cynicism.

"I will entertain no further questions. Simply watch. After all..."

His gaze swept over the Marquis with something approaching pity.

"That is the extent of your freedom."

Eratosthenes took his first step forward.

Reniar never managed to stop the old monk as he passed.

A wind stirred across the glassy lake.

A single fallen leaf drifted down, landing softly upon the water.

And dangerous ripples spread across the surface that had, until now, been perfectly still.

Valdina — The Strait of El Amuz

In the square, the banner of Valdina's royal house snapped in the wind.

Gray flagstones stretched beneath a merciless sun. Knights lined the perimeter in rigid formation, their polished armor blazing, their spears angled toward the sky like iron thorns.

A crowd had gathered—hastily assembled, restless with anticipation.

"Is it today? The day they release him?"

The whisper slithered through the assembly. Faces bore a mixture of cynicism and hungry curiosity. Some were mere spectators, drawn by the promise of spectacle. Others glared toward the platform with eyes that burned, searching for any target upon which to unleash their suppressed fury.

At the center of their attention, beneath the raised stage, knelt Raju.

Knights hauled him upward with rough hands.

CLANG. CLANG.

The iron chains around his ankles rang out with every movement—a sound like fate being measured link by link.

Raju lifted his head.

Sweat and dust stained his face. His hands were bound, his knees pressed to stone. And yet there was nothing of the condemned man about him—no trace of misery or defeat.

His spine was straight. His shoulders, square. And upon his lips... a smile.

He swept his gaze across the crowd and shook his head with open derision.

Say whatever you like. So long as Katzen stands behind me, not one of you can touch a hair on my head.

"Did you see that? The audacity!"

"They should have taken his head when they had the chance!"

Angry murmurs rippled through the crowd, falling perfectly into Raju's provocation.

At the front of the assembly stood the Claudio family.

As the bereaved—the family of the victim—they had been granted the privilege of proximity. Yet Raju found no trace of righteous anger in their faces.

Catherine's eyes met his, soft with sorrow.

'I will follow you. I will wait for you in Katzen.'

Raju read her silent words upon her lips, and for the first time, his smile warmed with genuine feeling. He inclined his head slightly—a gesture meant to reassure.

Catherine struggled to contain her tears, overwhelmed by relief and lingering dread. How she must have agonized over the possibility that something might go wrong—that Raju would be executed here, in Valdina, before he could escape.

Once today passes, we will be reunited in Katzen.

Nearby, Samon met Raju's gaze and offered a quiet nod.

Only days ago, they had exchanged secret correspondence. In those letters, Raju had disclosed the location and precise sums of his hidden slush funds, along with detailed plans for continuing Samon's smuggling operations.

Given the circumstances, accessing those funds immediately was impossible—too much scrutiny, too many watching eyes. But for Samon, who had lost his leg and now faced the prospect of losing his patron as well, Raju's letter had been a lifeline thrown into drowning waters.

Some would call me heartless. A son who feels nothing for his father's death—who bears no grudge against his murderer.

But his father had abandoned him first.

If his father had lived—if he had produced another heir through his own efforts—would Samon ever have had a chance?

He had seen his father's true nature over many years. The calculations were already complete.

So no—I care nothing for his wretched death, nor that Raju was the one who delivered it.

Raju's eyes continued to sweep the crowd.

Where are you, Birna?

Once his journey home began, he would not see his daughter for some time...

At last, he spotted a flash of pink hair.

With a heart aching for one final connection, Raju tried to catch her eye. But Birna remained lost in the press of bodies, her head bowed, never once looking up.

"It's coming! Look—it's here!"

A blue carriage appeared at the square's entrance.

It was enormous, magnificent, dripping with ornate decoration—a stark contrast to Valdina's tradition of restrained simplicity. The extravagance announced its origin at a glance: Katzen. The greatest empire on the continent.

Whether the display was meant to demonstrate imperial might or to mock the Valdinians, no one could say. Perhaps both.

The carriage halted. The door, emblazoned with the Katzen imperial crest, swung open.

A messenger emerged—robed in antiquated ceremonial garb, bearing the Order of the Red. He ascended the platform with measured dignity.

Raju watched him approach, joy swelling in his chest. Yet the messenger seemed consumed by his mission, never once glancing toward the prisoner.

FWOOSH—

The scroll unrolled with a sharp snap.

An unseasonably dry wind swept through the square, setting the messenger's black robes billowing—heavy, solemn, like the wings of an ill omen.

"I bear His Imperial Majesty's decree. Roberto Raju..."

Raju's smile widened. He nodded along to the messenger's words, drinking in his own name like fine wine.

His gaze drifted toward the distant villa—toward the royal palace where the Princess of Valdina was conspicuously absent.

Yes. What will you do now?

No matter how desperately Valdina struggled, they could never eclipse the majesty of the greatest empire overnight.

Raju wanted to laugh aloud. He felt drunk on triumph, as though he had personally slapped the Valdinian royal family across both cheeks.

He tried to contain it, but the corners of his mouth trembled—laughter threatening to burst forth like a dam about to break.

Then the messenger spoke.

"You will NOT
be repatriated to the Empire."

What?

Raju's eyes grew as wide as lanterns.
