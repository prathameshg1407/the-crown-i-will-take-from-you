The Prime Minister's
Office

"You're not enduring. You're acting."

Cesare watched from the window.

The Princess was shaking.

Her arms trembled violently as she forced herself to remain upright on the cold stone. Every breath looked like effort dragged through broken glass.

Three days, Cesare thought.
On a battlefield, she'd already be carrion.

Her body was failing—but her eyes were not.

Even from this distance, he could see it: the fire buried beneath exhaustion. Not defiance. Calculation.

Then movement.

A bulky figure waddled into the chapel courtyard, cloak fluttering uselessly in the wind. For a man his size, he moved with disturbing eagerness.

Cesare narrowed his eyes.

"You may not approach the Princess," a guard warned.

"Oh, relax. It'll take a moment," came the oily reply.

"Minister Etienne—this is inappropriate!"

Count Etienne brushed past them and stopped directly in front of Medea.

She didn't react. Didn't even lift her head.

"Pugh~ So this is the famous cursed Princess. Pitiful."

Something cold flickered behind Cesare's eyes.

Without turning, he plucked three small pebbles from a flowerpot. His fingers moved lazily.

Flick.

Thwack.

"E—EVIL!"

Etienne shrieked as his leg buckled. He crashed face-first into the mud with a wet, humiliating splatter.

"My leg! I've been attacked! Assassination—guards! GUARDS!"

The soldiers spun in circles, weapons raised. There was no one there.

Silence.

"What's happening?" Siseo asked, looking up.

"Nothing," Cesare replied blandly.

The pebbles rolled back into the pot.

The Palace Grounds

By the time Siseo left for the war council, the sky had broken.

Rain fell in sheets—cold, merciless, flattening the world into gray.

"Boss, wait here!" Gallo called, already soaked. "I'll bring the carriage!"

"It's unnecessary."

"Like hell it is—!"

But Cesare had already stepped into the rain.

Water soaked through his coat instantly. He didn't notice.

He stopped.

Under the statue, Medea knelt—drowned in white.

Her dress clung to her like a shroud. Rain and sweat streaked her face. Her breathing was shallow, uneven.

Her arm gave out.

She hit the stone hard enough to split skin.

Blood bloomed red—then vanished under the rain.

Cesare didn't move.

Do you even have human blood?

The curse. The accusation. The years of being surrounded by enemies who waited for him to fall.

I won't lose. I will endure.

Medea gasped, her arm collapsing again.

For a moment, she fell forward.

Cesare took a step before he realized it.

Then—

She rose.

Teeth clenched. Neck veins straining. She glanced—just once—toward the shadowed colonnade where Pinatelli stood watching.

Then she lowered her head again.

Submission. Perfectly performed.

Cesare stopped.

A faint smile curved Medea's lips.

"…Interesting. You're not enduring. You're acting."

She was harvesting pity. Turning suffering into leverage.

Talent, Cesare decided. Real talent.

Medea's vision swam.

Not yet. Hold on. Just a little longer.

Suddenly—the rain stopped.

Medea blinked.

Silence.

She looked up.

☂️

A black umbrella hovered above her.

No footsteps. No presence.

The courtyard was empty.

The Queen Mother's Palace

"She's vicious," the Queen Mother snapped, pacing. "Stubborn beyond reason. Why does she provoke me?"

Pinatelli wrung a warm cloth calmly.

"Do you remember the maid who begged to take the punishment?"

"That impertinent girl?"

"Her name is Neril. Former Royal Guard." Pinatelli paused. "She served your son."

The Queen Mother froze.

"To the Princess, losing that maid would mean losing her father again."

Silence.

The Queen Mother's eldest son—gone. The wound still raw.

"Hmph," the Queen Mother muttered. "John was a King. That's a ridiculous comparison."

But the venom was gone.

"When my husband died, I had years to grieve," Pinatelli said softly. "I was an adult. The Princess lost everything overnight."

"…Are you blaming me?" the Queen Mother asked.

"Never." Pinatelli bowed her head. "But may I ask something else?"

She straightened.

"Why did the Head Maid attack so viciously? Forging evidence. Destroying reputation. That wasn't discipline—it was annihilation."

The Queen Mother frowned. "She claimed it was for Medea's sake… though she was Catherine's friend."

Pinatelli stepped forward and produced a stack of documents.

"I reviewed her accounts."

The Queen Mother read.

Once. Twice.

Her hands began to shake.

Bribes

Embezzlement

Selling Secrets

The tea table overturned with a crash.

"Cuisine! That treacherous wretch!"

Outside, rain continued to fall.

But beneath one unseen umbrella,
a girl still knelt—
waiting for the final piece to fall into place.
