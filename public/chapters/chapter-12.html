— The Truth Is Often Inconvenient —

“The truth is often inconvenient, Auntie.”

Medea’s voice was small and even, but it carried across the room like a blade.

Catherine blinked, flustered. The timid, foolish girl she remembered would never have answered back. She forced a practiced smile into place.

“I understand. I will bring the doctor later… and we can speak privately.”

Medea knew that Catherine would never actually take an Imperial doctor to the Dowager. It had been a bluff—one Catherine had folded.

“Medea!”

Birna stamped her foot, pouting.

“Mother went all the way to Kazen for you! Personally! Isn’t it an insult to treat her efforts like that?”

“Auntie left herself?” Medea raised a brow. “In wartime? I didn’t think Lord Siseo would grant such a trip.”

Catherine flushed.

“Birna—be quiet.”

She forced a smile that trembled at the edges.

“All formalities were observed. A government delegation is arriving shortly; the doctor simply came ahead with me.”

A smooth lie.

Catherine dabbed at her eyes with a lace handkerchief and pressed the pity button for the room.

“You call me your daughter, yet you watched the Head Maid torment me for years. If Birna had been the one beaten and starved, would you be this composed?”

Catherine’s hand tightened on Medea’s. Her eyes sought the room for rescue; the maids looked on, sympathy turning thin and shocked.

“You knew. The rumors reached the Duke’s house, did they not? Why else were my maids replaced so swiftly?”

A pause. The new maids shifted. Catherine’s composure frayed; her practiced tears read more like guilt than grief.

Catherine reached forward, clinging.

“Medea, I—Quiggin is a longtime friend. I never meant—”

Medea pulled her hand back as if burned.

“Then forgive me. I cannot bear this theater.”

She rose slowly.

“If you loved me, Auntie, would you stand so calmly while your niece was humiliated?”

Catherine opened her mouth, found no words.

“You can say daughter all you like, but only blood decides whose face the court remembers. I have learned that lesson now.”

Medea left, the silence swallowing her steps.

Catherine slumped into a chair and snapped at Birna.

“You made it worse! I thought we had her—tamed her.”

“It’s Quiggin,” Birna muttered. “She went too far. I told her to be subtle.”

Catherine fumed, then slid into a quieter panic—reaching for explanations, excuses, anything to sew the threads back together.

— Administrative Wing, Late —

A single lamp threw long shadows.

“I heard you looked pitiful today,” a gravel voice observed. “Compared to the rumors, you held up well.”

Count Etienne lounged against a pillar, the picture of oily solidity: wide, piggish face, leering grin.

“You crawling worm. Mind your mouth.”

“Oh come now,” Etienne chuckled. “The Duchess slapped you. She cut you loose.”

Quiggin’s mouth tasted of iron. She touched the inside pocket of her dress and felt the crushed scrap of paper there:

You have served well. A successor will be appointed. — Regent’s Office

A dismissal. A calculated exile.

She had been sacrificed to preserve the Regent’s reputation.

“The Regent wants no images of his niece abused leaking out. Do as you’re told. Maybe exile will spare your head.”

Quiggin’s face hardened into something small and lethal. Pride had been stripped; only survival was left.

She needed leverage. Fast.

“You have ears, don’t you? I have sources. Things that would make a minister blanch.”

“Such as?”

Quiggin let the invitation hang: not specific, but intimate enough to bait a toad.

“I’m going to the Dowager. But I will not go empty-handed. I need protection.”

“Protect yourself from the Duke? You expect me to stand against His Lordship for you?”

She let a pause do the convincing work.

“What if I offered you a path to something… greater?”

“Help me, and I can put you in a position where you bend the next succession.”

“The Princess.”

Etienne’s greedy imagination unfurled: lands, favors, influence.

“In the right hands, she could be a prize. Help me and the Regent will owe you. Or better—help me secure a bond with the royal line.”

“Do you realize what you’re proposing?”

Quiggin straightened, the spark of desperation in her eyes like flint.

“I realize I have nothing left to lose. And I would rather sell my soul than watch them throw me away.”

Silence settled as the two predators measured their angles.

“If you’re serious… We’ll talk.”

“Good. We begin at dawn.”

“Medea.”

Outside, the palace slept under a cold, indifferent moon.
Inside, alliances were silently reassembled like knives being sharpened.
