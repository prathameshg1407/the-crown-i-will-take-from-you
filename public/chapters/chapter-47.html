Chapter Continuation

The Second
Arm

"I find it best to address rot before it spreads."

Medea watched the Claudios retreat.

The Regent had fled.
Catherine was chasing her daughter through side corridors like a panicked handler after a loose animal.

The banquet hall—once meant to be a snare—now belonged to her.

One arm cut off, Medea thought, her gaze drifting to the corner where Minister Etienne drank alone, his laughter too loud, his movements too careless.

Now for the other.

"Your Highness."

The voice came from her right.

Medea turned.

Margrave Montega stood before her, his broad frame casting a long shadow in the candlelight. His expression was unreadable, carved from years of war and disappointment.

"Margrave," Medea inclined her head slightly. Not a bow. A recognition.

Montega studied her—really studied her—for the first time.

Not the rumors.

Not the caricature.

The girl standing before him.

"You planned this," he said quietly.

It wasn't an accusation.

It was a statement of fact.

"I prepared for it."

Medea met his gaze without flinching.

Montega exhaled slowly through his nose.

"I marched for seven days through starving villages to reach the capital. I expected decadence. I expected cowardice."

His eyes flicked briefly toward the empty dais Joaquin had abandoned.

"I did not expect this."

The fireworks' afterimage still burned faintly against the windows.

"The soldiers will talk. They will say Saint Esther answered Valdina tonight."

"That is enough," Medea replied. "Hope feeds armies longer than bread."

A corner of Montega's mouth twitched. Not a smile. Something closer to reluctant respect.

"You speak like a commander."

"I learned from watching them die," Medea said evenly.

Silence stretched between them.

Finally, Montega bowed.

Deep. Unmistakable.

"To the King's sister. And to Valdina."

Around them, nobles pretended not to stare—but the ripple spread fast.

Montega had chosen his side.

Across the hall, Minister Etienne felt it.

He drained his glass too quickly and nearly choked.

Something is wrong, he thought, wiping his mouth with his sleeve. Very wrong.

The Duke had fled.

Montega had bowed.

And the Princess—who was supposed to be a decoration—now stood at the center of gravity.

Etienne's gaze slid back to Medea.

Her black dress drank the light.

Her eyes reflected it.

A chill crept up his spine.

That girl is looking at me.

"Your Highness," Etienne called, forcing a smile as he waddled forward. "What a… inspiring evening. Truly. Your devotion to His Majesty moved us all."

Medea turned fully toward him.

Up close, Etienne smelled of wine and sweet rot. His pupils were slightly dilated. His hands trembled—just a little.

Four months, she thought. The halus is working.

"I am glad you enjoyed it, Minister. I was hoping the court would feel united tonight."

"Of course! Unity!" Etienne laughed too loudly. "We are all one family here."

"One family," Medea repeated.

She opened her fan halfway—slow, deliberate.

"Then you won't mind if I ask you to stay a little longer tonight. I intend to speak with several ministers. About… internal matters."

Etienne's smile stiffened.

"Internal… matters?"

"Yes," Medea said, eyes bright and merciless. "I find it best to address rot before it spreads."

The word rot landed like a blade.

Etienne swallowed.

"O-of course. Anything for the Royal Family."

Behind him, Umberto poured another glass of wine—his hands steady, his eyes downcast.

Good boy, Medea thought. Obedient. Afraid.

The hall buzzed with celebration, with faith, with false miracles.

But beneath the music and laughter, something else moved.

The second arm was already bleeding.

Medea closed her fan.

The night was far from over.

End of Scene
