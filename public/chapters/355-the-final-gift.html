Chapter 255

The Final Gift

"Now, the throne where you will sit is clean."

Had Perdiccas II not harbored such malicious intent toward the First Prince and Princess, Eratosthenes might have granted him a quieter, slower death.

"I cannot watch you smear your filth upon those children."

Eratosthenes thought of the First Princess.

He thought of what she was trying to protect, and the people sheltered within that circle.

He himself would never stand inside that protective boundary—but Cesare, that boy, certainly would.

That conviction drove Eratosthenes to his decision without hesitation.

This was all he could offer in a relationship that could never truly be called family.

It will be the first and last gift I can give.

"Chief attendant! Is anyone out there?"

The moment Eratosthenes fulfilled his decades-long vengeance, all of Perdiccas II's hopes shattered.

The Emperor stumbled toward the door with desperate urgency.

"Call the palace physician—urgh! No, summon the divine healers—cough! No, fetch the court wizards! Quickly!"

But all the servants had been dismissed long ago.

Before he'd taken three steps, the Emperor pitched forward.

Blood poured from his mouth, spreading across the golden carpet in a dark pool.

"Help... me...!"

His breathing grew labored. His hand clawed at empty air, as though trying to grasp something— anything—to anchor himself to life.

That reaching hand fell limp.

With eyes still wide in disbelief, Perdiccas II died.

In the end, he perished before ever reaching the door.

Dark crimson blood and bile trickled from the corners of his mouth.

Nowhere on that face—frozen in shock, horror, and despair—remained even a trace of imperial majesty.

The end of one who'd driven countless souls into suffering and death proved pitifully brief and wretched.

"Ha... how pathetic."

Did he truly spend decades preparing, only for it to end in an instant?

"Eleanor... your father was a fool."

If only he'd acted sooner, he might have seen her again that much earlier.

All that remained in the silent chamber was the bitter laughter of a collapsing old man.

"Your Highness! Your Highness!"

Cesare's horse was just approaching the palace gates when attendants rushed forward, breathless and desperate.

"Your Highness, you must not leave!"

They blocked his path, even as his black stallion reared threateningly.

"Your Highness, please...! You must come to the Emperor's chambers. Immediately!"

These wore the same palace livery as the servants he'd dismissed earlier.

Though they shrank with fear and lowered their voices, they refused to yield.

Cesare turned his head, his cold gaze demanding explanation.

"His Majesty... His Majesty has been murdered."

Moments ago, a servant had entered the too-quiet Emperor's chamber and discovered Perdiccas II's corpse.

"You summon me, and now you want to frame me for his death?"

Derision filled his cold eyes.

"It seems you wish to pin the Emperor's murder on me. Tell whoever sent you not to provoke me further. I'm already restraining myself from leading an army to raze this palace to the ground."

The killing intent radiating from him felt almost tangible.

"No! Truly! Please, believe me! His Majesty is... he's dead!"

Cesare wheeled his horse around without the slightest hesitation.

It had to be false.

This was the Imperial Palace of Katzen, the very heart of the empire.

Who would dare venture into the Emperor's own chambers to kill him?

"Wait—Lord Eratosthenes...!"

Before they could finish, Cesare's cloak whipped behind him as he spurred his mount forward.

The moment Cesare entered the Imperial chambers, the thick scent of blood assaulted his senses.

Perdiccas II lay sprawled on the floor.

Ashen complexion. Robes drenched in blood.

His rigidified face bore expressions of terror and fury—clear evidence he'd never anticipated such an end.

The scepter in his hand was stained crimson.

"You've arrived, Cesare."

Cesare's head snapped toward the voice.

An elderly man stood beside the golden throne.

His gray suit was soaked with blood, darkened almost to black.

Cesare looked between the faded old man and the fallen corpse.

Blood dripped from the hem of Eratosthenes' robes—but it wasn't Perdiccas II's.

"Why..."

Shock filled his golden eyes.

This outcome had never appeared in any of his calculations.

He'd never imagined Eratosthenes would choose this ending.

"Why would you..."

The final words caught in his throat, swallowed by labored breathing.

Why did you choose this path?

I know better than anyone that the revenge you waited for wasn't supposed to take this form.

Eratosthenes—a man who wouldn't tolerate a single speck of dust on his honor, whose pride defined him.

For what reason did you stain your own pride and honor by bloodying your hands directly?

"Why... you..."

Eratosthenes paid him no mind, instead using the clean portion of his robe to wipe blood from the throne.

Though his body swayed with exhaustion, he worked meticulously, as if polishing the throne were his final sacred duty.

Swish. Swish.

In this manner, even the last traces of blood Perdiccas II had coughed up in his dying moments were erased without memorial.

The throne gleamed golden once more.

It was difficult to believe a tragedy had just unfolded.

The Inheritance

"Now, the seat where you will reign is clean."

Cesare's lips parted, but no words emerged.

In truth, he'd already guessed the answer.

He understood why this old man had shouldered all the stigma.

If he hadn't... I would have killed Perdiccas with my own hands.

"Come here."

Eratosthenes, looking utterly spent, leaned against the base of the throne and extended his hand.

Step. Step.

Cesare's pace quickened—almost breaking into a run.

As the distance closed, Cesare's shadow fell across the elderly man.

A faint smile touched the corner of Eratosthenes' mouth.

"When did time pass so swiftly..."

That small child who'd barely reached his waist had grown to tower over him in what felt like an instant.

"No."

Cesare dropped to his knees.

"I don't accept you leaving like this."

His face remained expressionless, his voice devoid of inflection.

But only Eratosthenes—who'd raised him with his own hands—would notice the tremor at the end of those words.

This boy is absurdly like me. He'd rather die than show weakness.

"It's... useless."

Eratosthenes smiled gently and pushed away the vial Cesare drew from his robes.

Blue holy water shimmered within the glass container.

Having spent his entire life preparing to kill Katzen's supreme ruler, he'd ensured he used poison that no holy water could reverse.

"I... only..."

Eratosthenes drew a deep, rattling breath, struggling to find words for Cesare.

Yet none seemed adequate.

Time doesn't return. Vanished moments can never be reclaimed.

"I don't... regret anything. Not... a single... thing."

I avenged my daughter. I raised you as though you were my grandson.

And I spared you from becoming the one who killed your father.

Trembling, gnarled hands fumbled within his robes.

He tried to retrieve something, but lacked the strength. His movements grew weaker.

With one last effort, he finally pulled an object from his breast.

"Here..."

With a wry smile, Eratosthenes attempted to wipe the item clean against his suit.

But the fabric was already saturated with blood.

He gave up, curling his fingers protectively around it instead.

It seemed even heaven was unwilling to grant him this final apology.

"I'm sorry... for everything."

Hah...

With his last exhalation, his breathing ceased.

Cesare caught the old man's collapsing body.

Clatter, roll.

From Eratosthenes' weakened grip, something tumbled free.

What Was Never Forgotten

Though stained with blood, it still held a faint luster.

The carved wooden toy was identical in shape to the one Eratosthenes had made for Cesare years ago—the one that still sat in his chambers.

The only difference: this one looked much newer than Cesare's time-worn treasure.

As though he'd been carving the same toy over and over again, year after year.

Unable to forget the affection he'd once given.

Cesare had never been alone.

Silence hung in the blood-stained chamber.

Cesare's throat worked violently, as though swallowing something sharp and unbearable.

On the back of his hand—where he gripped the hem of Eratosthenes' cooling robes—veins stood out in harsh relief.

He said nothing.

Made no sound.

But his shoulders trembled almost imperceptibly as he cradled the body of the only father he'd ever truly known.

End of Chapter 255
