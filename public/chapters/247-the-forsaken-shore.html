Chapter 247

The Forsaken
Shore

"This is a land abandoned by the gods. We stay only because we cannot die."

The shabby dock materialized through the morning mist as Gallo lowered a parchment bearing the Facade's emblem.

"Boss, Your Highness—this is the only settlement in the region."

True to his word, the entrance to a small village emerged beyond the weathered pier. It looked less like a town and more like a collection of structures that had simply refused to collapse.

"The village is modest, but it serves as a waystation for rangers and hunters traversing the Swamp of Gluttony. We should replenish our supplies here."

The provisions prepared aboard ship would suffice for most needs, but fresh hay and clean drinking water for the horses could not be conjured from storage holds.

"We must move as quickly as possible."

Cesare inclined his head in agreement with Medea's assessment.

"Gallo, complete the resupply within half a day. We enter the forest at first light."

"Understood, Boss."

Cesare turned to address a contingent of his men.

"Eta through Kappa—remain here and secure the rear. Establish a defensive position."

"But Your Highness—"

"We cannot bring too large a force to the canyon. Numbers will only draw unwanted attention."

By now, the Holy Kingdom would surely be aware of Medea's disappearance. They would not simply allow the oracle's designated sacrifice to slip away unchallenged.

"The temple's eyes are everywhere."

The corners of Cesare's mouth curved upward as his gaze settled on the decrepit chapel roof rising above the village fence.

'The Holy Kingdom will likely dispatch the Knights Templar to pursue us.'

Medea had anticipated the temple's response. Rachel and High Priest Paul, in particular, would spare no effort to recapture her.

It did not matter.

'By the time the Knights Templar arrive here, I will have returned to Valdina.'

With the corpse of an evil dragon.

The Edge of the Swamp

The village squatted in the rugged terrain at the border of the Swamp of Gluttony—less a proper settlement than a place where civilization had come to die.

A fence of black logs and thorny branches encircled the perimeter. Rusted iron scraps and sharpened bone fragments jutted from the barrier at irregular intervals—crude but effective deterrents.

"Your Highness, security here appears questionable at best. Please exercise caution."

Neril straightened her posture and whispered the warning.

In terrain this harsh, few chose to settle willingly. Most residents were fugitives from justice, rangers and hunters who stalked demonic prey, or individuals with compelling reasons to keep their identities hidden.

Medea nodded absently.

"It's fine. And Neril—you cannot address me that way here any longer."

"...Indeed, Neril. If Lord Kensington knew how poorly you adapt to irregular circumstances, he would have abandoned you long ago."

Tom joined in with a grin, his freckles clustering together as his face creased with amusement.

"'Little Neril' has a charming ring to it, don't you think? Trust me on this."

He looked every inch the novice hunter—leather armor scuffed and worn, a hatchet dangling from a loosely knotted rope at his hip.

Medea had likewise adopted the guise of a ranger.

A whip, a crossbow, a dagger... She had armed herself with everything.

Cesare nearly laughed aloud when he took in her attire.

Beneath her heavy robes, she appeared to have doubled in size from the sheer weight of weapons she carried.

"Dea."

Medea lifted her gaze at the sound of her name.

With her thick hood, her hair bound invisibly beneath it, and her cheeks faintly flushed from the dry sea breeze, she reminded Cesare of a young seedling just breaking through the soil.

This was likely her first sea voyage. He felt a pang of sympathy imagining how her small frame had endured ten days of rough swells.

Medea, however, appeared entirely unaffected.

"Gallo has secured lodgings. Rest there for a while. Once we leave this place, conditions will become far more demanding."

Cesare reached out and gently tugged the hem of her hood, pulling it lower.

Medea's brow furrowed as her head sank deeper into the fabric.

When she looked up again, Cesare had vanished—Terence in tow.

No one—not even Peleus—had ever treated her quite like this.

Others either revered Medea or believed in her schemes and sought to assist her.

But Cesare sometimes treated her like a young child who still required looking after. He was the only person in the world who did so.

"Wait here, alright? Can you manage that?"

The way he patted her hand soothingly, or played pranks that even a ten-year-old would find juvenile—

"Ha...!"

Medea felt equal parts embarrassed and strangely moved.

"The forest, you say? The pitch-black forest? More fools marching off to die."

The innkeeper Gallo had found was a middle-aged man with a bristling beard that seemed to consume half his face.

Like the gray town itself, both the inn and its proprietor exuded an air of irreversible decay.

Sunken eyes. Bloated features. A jagged scar across one cheek. Every line spoke of a life carved by hardship.

"Master, isn't that rather harsh treatment for guests who've already paid handsomely for their rooms? A word of encouragement wouldn't go amiss."

"I could fill this dock ten times over with fools who shouted the same bravado before getting themselves killed."

Thud.

He slammed a bowl of stew onto the table with unnecessary force.

"Just last month, the evil dragon descended upon this village."

His eyes blazed with mingled despair and fury.

"My son. My mother. My pregnant wife."

Each word fell like a stone.

"They all perished—choking on its venom, coughing blood until they drowned in it. All because the beast spread its wings and cast its shadow over our homes!"

The innkeeper jabbed a finger toward the leaden sky and released a sound that was half-sigh, half-scream.

"Do you think there is life here? Do you?"

His voice cracked.

"One way or another, for those of us already dead inside, this is all that remains. We stay because we lack even the will to leave. We're simply existing—because we can't manage to die."

His tone was saturated with resignation.

"This is a land forsaken by the gods. Even the Holy Kingdom dares not touch it. If you don't wish to share our curse, cease your foolishness and turn back while you still can."

Having delivered his warning, the innkeeper deposited the remaining food and withdrew without another word.

"Ugh... how does one even respond to that?"

Gallo scratched his head and cast a sidelong glance at Medea, concerned that the innkeeper's words—practically a curse—might have unsettled her.

But Medea's expression remained unchanged.

The Dragon's Shadow

"Gallo. Commit this to memory."

Though the innkeeper's brutal account of the dragon might have shaken another, Medea had found a crucial detail within his words.

"The evil dragon was born from remnants of the Primordial Darkness. When you step into its shadow, you absorb the essence of that darkness."

"Your limbs will grow numb. Your senses will dull as though you've been poisoned. Under no circumstances must you enter the dragon's shadow. Relay this warning to the others."

She handed him a parchment listing the supplies required for the expedition.

Her characteristically composed expression was no different from when she had stood in the halls of the Valdinan Palace.

"Your Highness—I mean—how do you know all this?"

The venom of the evil dragon was knowledge even Gallo had only acquired after arriving in this forsaken place.

"Did you imagine I had embarked on this venture unprepared?"

When the corners of Medea's lips curved upward, Gallo looked uncharacteristically flustered.

"Let the horses rest and give them as much water as they can drink. We'll gather supplies and return shortly."

With that, Medea and her small party vanished into the narrow streets.

Gallo stood alone, wearing a sullen expression.

"Why am I always the one stuck with the menial tasks?!"

The village streets proved equally unsettling.

Though it was midday, the fog hung so thick that one could not tell whether the sun still shone above.

Most shops were shuttered tight, their windows dark, their doors barred. There were no merchants hawking wares, no voices calling out to passersby.

"What exactly are you searching for, miss?"

"Everything."

Medea, who had been scanning the streets with careful attention, turned abruptly into an alleyway.

Threading through side streets and backways, they encountered more travelers than they had seen on the main thoroughfare.

After walking for some time, Medea halted before a dilapidated storefront.

"Here. This one. If we wish to avoid unnecessary complications."

Creak.

The door opened with a protesting groan.
