Chapter 240

The Bride of
Ancient Darkness

"When gods demand sacrifice, mortals must choose: obey, or defy."

"Fire and ash... Does this portend some great calamity befalling the continent?"

"Valdina's daughter... Medea? How could the Princess—the very sister of Emperor Valdina—be offered to the Evil Dragon?"

Murmurs of disbelief rippled through the assembled clergy.

In the beginning, when the Goddess extinguished the Primordial Darkness, certain remnants had escaped complete purification.

Over countless ages, these fragments condensed and coalesced, eventually crystallizing into their purest, most potent form.

Thus was born the Evil Dragon.

The last surviving darkness. A sacred curse made manifest.

Throughout history, the Evil Dragon would emerge upon the continent without warning. Each time its vast wings unfurled to blot out the sky, devastation followed in its wake.

Even the Holy Nation, with all its divine authority, dared not attempt purification. They could only contain the truth—keep the dragon's existence shrouded in absolute secrecy.

"According to this oracle, the Evil Dragon shall soon manifest upon the mainland once more."

Heavy sighs echoed through the chamber.

The joy that had bloomed moments before now withered into anxiety—a creeping dread, as though the apocalypse itself loomed just beyond the threshold.

"Your Eminence—look! The oracle... it continues!"

Fresh excitement erupted among the Priests of Records.

"There is more! The inscription is not yet complete!"

All eyes turned to the sacred stele, where new characters were carving themselves into existence beneath the original prophecy.

The Second Revelation

"Her fate is known only to those
chosen by Gods and Stars."

"In the name of the Bride,
the continent shall be delivered from destruction."

"'Her fate is known only to the chosen'? What precisely does this signify...?"

The High Priests exchanged uncertain glances, brows furrowed in bewilderment.

"Is it not obvious?!"

Paul's voice cut through their confusion like a blade.

"The meaning is plain as day! If we deliver Princess Valdina to the Evil Dragon, the entire continent shall be spared!"

His eyes blazed with fervent conviction.

I knew it. The Goddess's will aligns perfectly with my own assessment.

The chamber fell silent. No one dared challenge him.

"The Princess of Valdina has brought disgrace upon the Goddess and our sacred Church through her defiant spirit, her insolent words, her contemptible conduct."

Paul's voice swelled with righteous fury.

"Now, through this divine oracle, we are given the means to cleanse her sins!"

The oracle had transmuted doubt into certainty—and certainty into zealous conviction.

"The oracle has spoken! Surely this must be the only path forward!"

"Your Eminence, even so..."

A dissenting voice, trembling with moral anguish.

The oracle spoke of a"bride"—but every soul present understood the true meaning.

Sacrifice.

As servants of faith, how could they condemn a living person to death?

Uneasy silence descended upon the Chamber of Oracle.

And yet...

Their opponent was the Evil Dragon itself.

A curse so profound it threatened the very order established by the Goddess. A terror so absolute that even the Holy Nation could do nothing but conceal its existence from the world.

"Can we truly hope to vanquish such a creature? How many thousands—how many tens of thousands—would perish in so vague an endeavor?"

If the death of one woman—Medea de Valdina—could avert such catastrophe...

Could that sacrifice truly be called meaningless?

Fear seeped through the assembly like poison through water.

And that fear provided the perfect justification: they had no choice but to sacrifice another so that the rest might survive. Coexistence with the dragon was impossible. Eradication was beyond their power.

"It grieves me deeply... but there is no alternative."

One priest spoke at last, his voice heavy with reluctant acceptance.

"History has shown us, time and again, the price of ignoring divine oracles. We know well the fate that befalls those foolish enough to defy the Goddess's will."

"Precisely! What matters above all is that the oracle has specifically named Valdina's daughter as the bride—the one through whom salvation shall come!"

"Your Holiness, we implore you—render your decision."

Multiple voices joined in urgent chorus.

"We are but humble vessels, conveying the Goddess's will through her sacred oracle. It is not our place to judge right from wrong."

"Indeed, Your Holiness. There can be no exceptions to divine law! Now that the revelation has been proclaimed, we must follow established protocol—notify every nation and implement the decree without delay."

Silence.

Pope Benedict closed his eyes. A soft sigh escaped his lips.

When he opened them again, his expression remained utterly impassive.

"...So be it."

The assembly erupted into frenzied activity.

Scribes copied the inscriptions from the sacred stele with meticulous care. The words were then carved into wooden printing blocks.

Ink was applied. The oracle was pressed onto the temple's official parchment—again and again, producing dozens of identical copies.

"Make haste! Every moment counts!"

Clutching the replicated proclamations to their chests, envoys of the temple mounted their swiftest horses and departed for every corner of the continent.

The laurel wreaths pinned to their vestments—symbol of the Holy Nation's authority—glinted in the afternoon sun.

"Your Holiness, may the Goddess guide your path. Allow me to escort you inside."

But even after the last priest had departed, Pope Benedict remained motionless before the stele.

"Is something troubling you, Your Holiness?"

"...Tell me. Is this oracle truly Her will?"

His ancient eyes—weathered by decades of faith and politics alike—fixed upon the sacred stone.

Or rather, upon a specific detail within the inscription.

The surface where the original oracle had been carved was pitted and cracked. Yet the second revelation—the addendum that followed—was rendered in pristine, unbroken strokes.

Two oracles. Inscribed at different times.

The Pope stared at that fracture for a long, weighted moment.

Perhaps I have simply grown too old. Too suspicious. Too weak.

"...It is nothing. Let us return."

He turned and began the long walk back.

Behind him, the frozen air of the chamber seemed to congeal—as though even the stones themselves harbored secrets they dared not speak.

The Western Border of Katzen

Deep within a forest so dense that not a single ray of sunlight penetrated its canopy, Cesare crouched low and swept his fingers across the earth.

"Slippery little rats."

Terence exhaled in frustration.

"We tracked them so carefully, yet they sensed our approach and fled like ghosts."

Beside him, Gallo tapped the forest floor with his boot, examining the disturbed soil.

"Boss, the white mare arrived safely. Her Highness the Princess has already given her a name—Silva, apparently."

No response.

Gallo, long accustomed to being ignored, continued without missing a beat.

"You know, when a gentleman wishes to present a gift to a lady, there are conventions. Flowers. Jewelry. Poetry, perhaps."

He rubbed his arm with an exaggerated wince.

"Not a half-feral horse that tries to take your limb off. I'm still nursing the bite marks from that demon."

Still grumbling, Gallo plucked a handful of wildflowers from a nearby bush and arranged them into a crude bouquet. He thrust them toward Terence with a theatrical flourish.

"Don't wave that thing at me."

Terence recoiled, swatting Gallo's arm away.

"The horse only bites because you refuse to treat it properly. Did you come out here to work, or to play?"

"Still, Her Highness seems to have befriended the beast readily enough. Quite remarkable, actually."

Gallo's tone shifted to genuine puzzlement.

"How in the world did she manage to hang a bell around that creature's neck? The thing nearly killed three grooms."

Despite Terence's pointed glare, Gallo's mouth refused to stay shut.

"But Boss—speaking as a free man with no national allegiance—is it really wise to smuggle a Katzen purebred across the border like that?"

He scratched his chin thoughtfully.

"I mean, Princess Medea did save your life. But still. What happens when the Agema discover that Arion's bloodline has been gifted to Valdina? If the Emperor finds out, he'll have a fit."

"Then let him."

For the first time, Cesare deigned to respond—his voice utterly indifferent.

"That is precisely what I intend."

"I'll take that as a good sign..."

Years of experience had taught Gallo to interpret his master's cryptic utterances.

He sidled closer to Terence and dropped his voice to a conspiratorial whisper.

"Why is the Boss suddenly so invested in fostering peace between two nations that have been at each other's throats for generations? One doesn't simply transform into a dove of diplomacy overnight."

His eyes sparkled with barely contained curiosity.

"This goes beyond repaying a life debt. There's something else at play here. Something he's keeping from us."

"I suspect I understand perfectly well. You, on the other hand..."

Terence smirked.

"All those years of debauchery have dulled whatever wits you once possessed."

"Brother, if you're going to insult me, at least give me the answer first—"

Gallo's complaint died in his throat.

His expression contorted—mischief dissolving into horror.

Before them lay a mountain of carcasses.

Dozens. Perhaps hundreds. Possibly more—the heap was too vast to count.

Above the grotesque mound, black smoke coiled lazily into the canopy, carrying with it a stench so vile it seemed to coat the inside of the lungs.

"What in all the hells..."

Terence's face twisted in revulsion.

Now they understood the unnatural silence that had deepened with every step into the forest's heart.

Rotting flesh. Exposed bone. The occasional human skull, scattered among the remains of beasts. All of it arranged in deliberate patterns, blocking their path forward.

The scene conjured memories of the Quiqueg—the cannibal tribe Cesare had annihilated years ago. That same ritualistic horror. That same calculated malevolence.

Black magic demands equivalent exchange. And the darker the sorcery, the greater the sacrifice required to bend the laws of reality.

What could they possibly have sought to gain by offering up so much life?

"They were invoking the Primordial Darkness."

Cesare stepped forward, his composure unbroken even before this tableau of death.

As one who had once embodied that ancient shadow himself, he could sense its residue—traces of darkness woven through the magic circle and the sacrificial altar before him.

"Vile and wicked! Even demons would recoil from such blasphemy! The great Primordial Darkness shall not forgive—"

A faint crease appeared between his elegant brows. A flicker of pain—sharp and sudden—lanced through his chest before subsiding.

Damn. The body still remembers.

His heart—once calcified by the Primordial Darkness—recognized and reacted to the deep scars left behind.

He had survived. But he was not entirely free of the aftermath.

Terence, oblivious to the momentary spasm of pain that had crossed his friend's face, spoke with grim seriousness.

"Did they use the Primordial Darkness to curse someone? The same way they cursed you?"

"I would prefer that."

Cesare's reply came flat and cold.

"A single death would end it cleanly."

For a man who had fought the curse until his final breath, the answer carried the weight of bitter experience.

"My Lord!"

Alpha burst through the undergrowth, crashing through branches in his haste.

In his hand, he clutched a sheet of cream-colored parchment.

"What's gotten into you? You look like you've seen a ghost."

Gallo, puzzled by Alpha's ashen complexion, reached out and took the paper.

He unfolded it.

Thud.

The wildflower bouquet slipped from his nerveless fingers. Gallo stood frozen, ice spreading through his veins, his eyes fixed on Cesare.

"What is it now?"

Terence approached with mounting suspicion.

"A dispatch from the Holy Nation."

Alpha's voice was barely above a whisper.

"An oracle has descended."

Oracle?

Cesare's striking features sharpened instantly.

"Your Highness, the Princess—"

Before Alpha could finish, Cesare crossed the distance in three swift strides and snatched the parchment from Gallo's paralyzed grip.

The paper—bearing the stamped pattern of the sacred stele—crumpled violently in Cesare's fist.

"I'm going to Valdina."

"Right now."
