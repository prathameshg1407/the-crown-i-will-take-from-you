Chapter 288

The Boy Who Learned
Not to Hope

"The cruelest lesson a child can learn is that love is conditional—and that some conditions can never be met."

Madame Tollin was startled by the young Princess's directness—a question no one else had dared to voice.

Yet after a moment's pause, she answered honestly.

The tangled relationship between the Prince, his adoptive mother, and his birth mother was a subject most chose to avoid entirely.

"The world claims that Her Majesty the Empress died because of His Highness Cesare. But in truth, the opposite is closer to the mark. His Highness delayed Her Majesty's decision for years."

The former Empress—Cesare's birth mother—had been an ambitious woman.

Her relationship with her brother-in-law, Perdiccas II, had been founded on cold calculation rather than passion. It mattered little who the father was; if the child born of her body one day sat upon the throne, her power would remain secure.

But before Perdiccas II could ascend to the throne himself, she died of puerperal fever shortly after giving birth to Cesare.

Compared to the grand ambitions she had nurtured, it was a sudden, meaningless death—one even she had not anticipated.

"The Empress will care for this child. Raise him as your own—treat him as though he were born of your body."

Perdiccas issued the command as though it were a gift to the wife he had betrayed countless times.

The wail of a newborn echoed through the Empress's chambers. Beside the cradle stood Eleanor, frozen in place.

No one tended to the infant.

One day passed. Then another.

The child's cries grew weaker.

Unable to feed, he vomited thin white liquid in helpless spasms. Eleanor watched with eyes devoid of feeling.

Though he was only a newborn, she could already see traces of her husband in those distinct features. The legendary beauty of the former Empress—celebrated as the fairest woman on the continent—was unmistakable as well.

Eleanor laughed softly.

"Their traces are written all over you."

She reached out. Her hands, withered like winter branches, extended toward the infant's throat.

At that moment, tiny fingers closed around her index finger.

Golden eyes—clear as morning light—fixed upon her face.

Eleanor stopped.

In those innocent eyes, she saw her own reflection. And she realized, with sudden clarity, that her life had grown as small and insignificant as this helpless creature's.

From that moment, she no longer wished to kill him.

"But she could never love His Highness."

Madame Tollin's voice grew heavy.

"As the child grew, he came to resemble his parents more and more. Saving his life was the greatest affection the Empress could bring herself to give."

She reached for her handkerchief, dabbing at her eyes.

"And His Highness Cesare... he understood that. Far too early."

She had witnessed the exact moment when the innocent light in the boy's eyes began to dim.

The moment when his hope for love vanished. When he learned that he could rely on no one.

It had come far, far too soon.

"He is lonely, Your Highness."

Madame Tollin's voice cracked.

"The world says His Highness has no blood in his veins, no tears in his eyes. But no one knows the truth—that his heart is greater than anyone imagines."

He was nothing like his father.

Cesare understood that Eleanor could not love him. And still, he considered her family.

"After the Empress... after she departed this world, His Highness protected all of us. Even though it was immediately after his triumph—even though his own position remained precarious."

The suicide of a member of the imperial family—especially an Empress—was considered a profound disgrace.

Perdiccas II convinced himself that his wife had died specifically to expose his failings. His rage fell upon the Empress's handmaidens, her father's household, anyone connected to her memory.

While everyone else averted their eyes from Eleanor's death, only Cesare refused to look away.

He laid her body in the imperial sanctuary with full honors and held a state funeral befitting an Empress.

He rescued the handmaidens who had been sentenced to execution. He shielded Eratosthenes—the Empress's father—and his disciples from the Emperor's wrath.

"Despite His Majesty's fury, despite fierce opposition from every quarter, His Highness refused to bend. Even though the triumphal celebration he had earned with his own blood was ruined as a result—he did not yield."

It was as though he sought to repay Eleanor for enduring a lifetime of humiliation—for being forced to raise another woman's child while her husband betrayed her again and again.

"Those who claimed to be his family pushed him away. Those who wanted to use him cared only for his bloodline. And His Majesty grew ever more suspicious of his son as Cesare grew stronger."

From the environment in which he was raised—where warmth was always withheld—to the constant threat of assassins' blades aimed at his throat...

It was only natural that Cesare became cold toward people.

Yet Madame Tollin could not forget the image of the boy who had once yearned so desperately for affection.

Even Eleanor—whom he had considered his only family—could not accept him in the end.

The warmth Cesare offered was never returned. Not by anyone.

A wrinkled hand reached out to clasp Medea's.

The world had been stunned to witness the cold-blooded First Prince lavishing affection upon his wife without reservation.

But Madame Tollin felt only pure, uncomplicated joy.

"Now that His Highness has found a true family... I could not be happier."

He would no longer stand alone against the world's bitter winds.

***

Darkness had fallen.

Medea gazed up at the sky. Her first day in Katzen was drawing to a close, fading like any other.

"Your Highness, the Lord sends word that he will be delayed and asks that you retire without waiting for him."

Even as night wrapped its cloak around the world, Cesare had not returned.

The bed they were meant to share stretched vast and empty before her—so spacious she could have rolled from one end to the other.

"Now that His Highness has found a true family, I could not be happier."

Remembering the old woman's words—the undisguised joy, the profound relief in her voice—Medea had found herself unable to respond.

This marriage was not real.

She was the one who would eventually have to leave his side.

When one crossed the ice wall that others dared not approach, the truth became clear.

Cesare Katzen was no monster without blood or tears.

Rather, he struggled constantly to preserve what was good within himself.

He repaid grudges tenfold—but he repaid kindness with equal fervor.

Just as he had fulfilled his duty to the adoptive mother who could never love him, he now gave his heart to Medea—the woman who had saved him from his cursed fate.

But she could not return this tender-hearted man's affection.

She could not love him. She could not remain at his side for the rest of her life.

She had always known this. Yet she did not think she had ever felt quite so selfish as she did today.

"His Highness is a lonely person."

Fragments of memory from three years past surfaced unbidden.

The confrontation that had thrust her into Katzen's political arena. Cesare finding her in the hunting grounds when she had nowhere left to hide.

Throughout those desperate months of struggle, he had been there. Always.

Especially in the moments when she felt most alone.

Back then, she had told herself she could trust no one—that she alone must carry the weight of her burdens.

"Is that why you never left me alone?"

Perhaps, in her—a woman who belonged nowhere, whom no one would shelter—he had seen himself.

The child he had once been.

A chill breeze slipped through the window, stirring Medea's silver hair.

The night air of Katzen was far colder than Valdina's. And it was not merely the temperature.

The harshness of this lonely, unforgiving land remained branded upon Medea's memory.

How long did he endure alone in this desolate place? How many years did that boy—still just a child—face this bitter cold without anyone beside him?

Almost unconsciously, Medea rose and wandered toward the window.

Through the glass, she saw only darkness. Cesare had still not returned.

The emptiness felt unbearable.

She drifted aimlessly through the bedroom until, without quite intending to, she found herself in Cesare's space.

Some of the items arranged on his table were familiar.

The dagger he would flip idly between his fingers—or hurl at Gallo when the man annoyed him. Tie pins hung from neatly pressed garments. A sachet that released the subtle fragrance of musk.

The objects he used every day lay within easy reach.

All were of the finest quality. Yet they seemed untouched—arranged with a precision that bordered on coldness, as though no human hand had ever disturbed them.

Then her gaze caught upon something.

A small ring, suspended from a delicate chain.

"...Eleanor."

Medea read the name inscribed upon it.

It had belonged to the deceased Empress.

In this austere room—where nothing carried sentiment, where no object held meaning—this was the single exception.

And it was placed where his hand could reach it at any moment.

So this is how he remembers his mother.

Medea set the ring down with careful reverence.

After I leave... will he remember me like this?

Somewhere in this cold, indifferent space, will something remain that once knew my touch?

She did not want to leave a scar upon his heart—not like the one the dead Empress had carved there.

And yet...

Medea swallowed a silent sigh as she recognized her own contradiction.

Even knowing she should not, she found herself hoping—foolishly, selfishly—that someday, even if only rarely...

She might be reflected in his memory.
