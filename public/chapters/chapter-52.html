The Collision

Bull in a
Thunderstorm

"You interfered."

The Attack

Etienne charged like a bull in a thunderstorm, breath ragged, face flushed with drink and fury.

"DIE!"

he screamed.

Medea did not flinch.

She tightened her grip on the Damascus fan until the steel ribs sang.

The strike she measured would have ended him—

one clean, clinical blow to the solar plexus,

and the toad's scream would be cut off mid-air.

Then

iron moved.

Cesare's arm slid around her waist as if she were featherlight.

In one motion he plucked her from the line of the charge, spun, and used his hip to redirect Etienne's momentum.

The Minister's path kinked—

a brutal kick to the ankle,

the man overbalanced—

and, by the smallest fraction of a degree, his trajectory aimed straight at Birna.

The Wreckage

The collision was grotesque and immediate.

Birna's shriek was high and thin.

She tried to flee; her heel caught the hem of the silver train.

The dozen diamonds embedded in the weave shredded the fabric like brittle paper.

Rrrrip.

Etienne barreled into her.

The refreshment table exploded:

crystal chimneys toppled,

cakes collapsed into frosting avalanches,

wine glass rain painted the floor.

Birna slid beneath the wreckage, glittering jewelry embedded in cream.

The dress lay in tatters; the girl lay half-exposed, eyes wide and stunned.

Beyond Shame

Etienne, however, was beyond shame.

Whatever drugs and humiliation had unstitched his mind now saw only motion.

He seized Birna's arm with a greasy, slavering greed.

"Pretty," he slurred. "Pretty—"

Samon lunged like a cornered dog, ripping at Etienne's shoulders.

The minister, delirious, bared his teeth and tried to match the violence with a drunken embrace, leaning toward Samon with a grotesque, groping insistence.

Guards piled in; it took four of them to wrench Etienne free.

He flailed like a drowning thing, finally yanked away, naked and foaming, his voice a raw howl that would echo in gossip for weeks.

Birna fainted,

the room scooped her up under tremulous hands;

Catherine screamed and threw a tablecloth over her daughter as if hiding the stain might hide the shame.

Chaos smelled like

wine and roses

and the sharp tang of ruined pride.

The Return

Cesare set Medea down as if she'd been nothing more fragile than a leaf.

He let go with a casual ease, an animal returning what it had held to the world.

"You're light," he said, voice gravelly behind the mask. "They starve you well."

Medea pushed a stray wet curl away from her cheek and looked up at him with a cold, composed patience.

Close enough now that he could smell the lemon on her skin, not close enough to read her.

"You interfered,"

she said.

"I saved you," Cesare answered.

He had a smile that never reached his eyes.

"I saw a woman about to be crushed and acted."

"You've become theatrical,"

Medea said.

Her fingers flexed around the fan; its steel glinted in the chandelier light.

He noticed the blade's intent the way a hunter notices the twitch of a rabbit's leg—

she had been ready to kill.

The realization sharpened something in his face:

amusement,

respect,

a long, slow curiosity.

He stepped back.

For a beat they simply measured one another.

Then Gallo, panicked and breathless, bounded up.

"Boss—I mean, Acares—what are you doing? You're supposed to be my muscle, not the Princess's babysitter!"

Cesare folded his arms.

"She looked like she needed help."

"Sir Gallo," she said with ice, "I didn't know you kept such a noisy wild dog. It barks in the wrong places."

Gallo blinked.

"She called you a dog."

Cesare let the corner of his mouth lift.

"She's interesting,"

he said simply.

Gallo, still off-balance, jabbed a finger at the ruined table.

"You kicked Etienne on purpose—sent him straight into the Claudio girl."

Cesare shrugged as if it were an inconvenience he hadn't meant to create.

His golden eyes slid back to Medea, predatory and amused.

"An accident," he said. "Or perhaps: a useful redirection."

Gallo snorted.

"If you were trying to save anyone, you'd have saved Lady Birna too."

Cesare's face did not warm. His tone dropped to something half prophecy, half joke.

"Stars need audiences,"

he said, the iron in his voice not hiding the edge.

"And every rising light asks for a shadow to shine against."

"Tonight the Venus burns—someone must be wasted so the rest can see it glow."

Medea's expression did not change.

She let him have his cryptic comfort, then turned and stepped away.

Around them the hall swam back into motion:

nurses fussing over Birna,

servants sweeping broken glass,

the Regent's face a hard mask of fury and humiliation.

Cesare watched her go,

the mask hiding a mouth that, for a moment,

almost smiled

like a man who'd discovered

a very promising new instrument.
