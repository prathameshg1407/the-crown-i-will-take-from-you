Chapter 292

The Arrow's
Declaration

"Some messages are best delivered at the tip of an arrow."

"Of course, Chamberlain. His Majesty's wellbeing takes precedence over all else."

Medea's smile was serene, her voice as gentle as morning light.

"It is only natural for a son to wish to see his father. We shall return at a more convenient time."

She reached into the folds of her gown and withdrew a small crystal vial.

"In the meantime, please deliver this to His Majesty—holy water blessed by the Holy Father himself. For his recovery."

Not a ripple of distress crossed her features. Her heart remained perfectly still.

I never expected Perdiccas II to receive us graciously.

The Emperor delighted in rattling his chains, in reminding those beneath him of their place. And she was not merely the First Prince's wife—she was the sister of Valdina's Emperor, a man Perdiccas II viewed as a perpetual thorn in his side.

How could he possibly resist the opportunity to humble me?

"Your Highness... such consideration for His Majesty... I am deeply grateful."

The old chamberlain bowed low, his relief palpable. The Crown Princess's composure had spared them all from an uglier scene.

It was then that voices drifted from a side door.

"Did you see how many arrows His Highness shot today?"

"More like how many His Majesty made him shoot. The Emperor insisted on bringing out his favorite bow."

Two young servants emerged, arms laden with arrows, wearing the livery of the Third Prince's household.

"At this rate, will His Highness ever have time for affairs of state?"

"Quiet! But truly... His Majesty gazes upon the Third Prince as though he were a nugget of pure gold."

Their laughter echoed thoughtlessly through the corridor.

"Ah."

Cesare exhaled—a soft sound, almost musical, yet threaded with something dark and restrained.

The chamberlain's shoulders drew up involuntarily. He had served long enough to recognize the particular quality of that sound—the calm that preceded a storm.

"Your Highness! The First Prince—!"

Before the warning could complete itself, Cesare moved.

In one fluid motion, he seized the nearest servant by the throat and lifted him clear off the ground. The man's feet dangled uselessly, his face cycling through confusion, terror, and the desperate need for air.

Without breaking stride, Cesare carried his burden toward the palace doors.

BOOM!

The doors exploded inward, torn from their hinges by the force of his kick.

The Imperial Garden

✦ ✦ ✦

At that moment, Perdiccas II was thoroughly absorbed in instructing his youngest son.

"Watch carefully, Juan. Your grip is still wrong. Hold it like this."

The Emperor had risen from his gilded seat to demonstrate personally, pressing his own bow into the Third Prince's hands.

"Truly, there is no one like Your Majesty."

Juan's eyes sparkled with manufactured wonder.

"Now I understand why they call this the Divine Palace. Such secrets of archery—I never dreamed they existed."

Thwack!

"A hit! His Highness strikes true again!"

The attendants burst into enthusiastic applause.

"Did you see, Father? I followed your instructions exactly, and it flew straight!"

"That's it. That's how it's done."

Perdiccas II nodded with undisguised satisfaction as Juan raised the bow for another attempt.

CRASH!

"Your Highness—!"

"What in—!"

Something massive hurtled through the air, demolishing the practice targets and scattering equipment across the immaculate lawn.

When the dust settled, the Third Prince's servant lay groaning amid the wreckage of fallen poles and splintered wood.

"First Prince! Have you forgotten where you stand? This is His Majesty's personal domain!"

The Western Empress's voice cracked like a whip.

But the words died in her throat as she registered the expression on Cesare's face—or rather, the terrifying absence of one.

"Cesare!"

Perdiccas II's roar echoed across the garden.

"It appears I have been away too long, Father."

Cesare's voice was silk drawn over steel.

"Long enough for you to become so... engrossed... in playing at war games with my brother."

His gaze, cold as winter iron, swept to Juan.

The Third Prince immediately dropped into a bow, his golden head dipping low.

"Greetings, Brother. And to you, Sister-in-law."

He had barely straightened before the Emperor seized his arm.

"The lesson is not finished. Juan, ignore the interruption. Your enemies will not pause for distractions on the battlefield."

Perdiccas II refused even to acknowledge the disturbance, his attention fixed deliberately on his younger son.

A soft voice cut through the tension.

"Medea de Katzen greets the imperial sun."

The Emperor's narrowed gaze finally crossed over Cesare to land upon the woman standing at his side.

She was smaller than he had expected. Pale skin like porcelain, silver hair that caught the light like spider silk, features so delicate they seemed liable to shatter at a touch.

Yet there was something in her bearing—something that did not bend.

Though she inclined her head in greeting, her knees remained unbowed. Her eyes met his directly, without flinching.

Insolent creature. Does she not know who stands before her?

The rumors had painted her as oblivious to court propriety. Perdiccas II now saw they had been generous.

"Continue, Juan."

Rather than acknowledge his daughter-in-law's greeting, the Emperor turned away as though she were no more significant than dirt beneath his heel.

The dismissal was deliberate. Absolute. A declaration that rang louder than any words could have.

Emperor and Empress alike devoted their entire attention to Juan's archery, as though the Crown Prince and his wife had simply ceased to exist.

Two strangers standing in their own family's garden. Invisible. Irrelevant.

Thwack!

Juan's arrow left the bowstring—

CRACK!

—and was cleaved perfectly in two, mid-flight.

The halves tumbled uselessly to the grass.

"...!"

Silence crashed over the garden like a physical weight.

All eyes turned to Cesare, who stood with an arm still extended, having plucked a discarded arrow from the ground and fired it in a single, effortless motion.

The implications settled over the court like frost.

Juan had used the Emperor's prized bow. Cesare had grabbed a random arrow from the dirt. Their shots had been fired at different times, from different positions.

And yet he had split his brother's arrow with surgical precision.

It was not a feat of archery. It was a message.

I could have killed him. I chose not to.

"Cesare, you insolent wretch!"

Perdiccas II's face had gone purple with fury.

"Did you just aim at your Emperor?"

"My deepest apologies, Your Majesty."

Cesare's tone held no apology whatsoever.

"I was concerned. With your weakened constitution, I feared perhaps your eyes and ears had failed you entirely. How fortunate that you can see me now."

He spun the remaining arrow between his fingers, almost lazily.

"And my wife."

"You dare—!"

The Emperor hurled his staff to the ground.

No one moved to retrieve it.

Not the Emperor's servants. Not the Third Prince's attendants. Not a single soul in that garden was willing to place themselves between Cesare and his target.

The arrow still rested between his fingers like a promise.

"Your Majesty. Empress."

Juan's voice broke the frozen silence, pitched carefully to soothe.

"My brother honors us with such a demonstration. To think that under Your Majesty's tutelage, he has achieved such mastery—truly, I have much to learn."

He turned and bowed deeply to Cesare.

"Forgive me, Brother. I did not know you were arriving today. Had I been aware, I would never have presumed to monopolize Father's time with my clumsy practice."

He scratched the back of his head with an expression of boyish embarrassment—golden hair catching the sunlight, smile warm and open, his bright uniform a stark contrast to Cesare's dark attire.

Light and shadow. Warmth and winter.

So this is why the nobles flock to the Third Prince.

That gentle, harmless face. That eager-to-please demeanor. Who would believe such a creature capable of threatening anyone?

"And Sister-in-law—do you enjoy archery as well? Though I suppose asking the woman who slew the evil dragon is rather foolish of me."

Juan's smile broadened as he addressed Medea directly.

"That was merely a fortunate stroke, achieved through His Highness the First Prince's strength. But yes, when time permits, I do practice with the bow."

"Wonderful!"

His face lit up with manufactured delight.

Behind that benevolent expression, Medea caught the flicker of calculation—the careful assessment of a predator determining whether its prey was worth the effort.

The Third Prince's methods are remarkably similar to my own.

Present a harmless facade. Observe. Wait. Then strike from behind when your opponent least expects it.

Rather than respond, she offered only a slight, enigmatic smile.

"The Emperor's Birthday Hunting Tournament approaches. Perhaps, if you have time, we might practice together beforehand—"

CRACK. CRACK. CRACK.

The sharp sound of splintering wood cut him off.

In Cesare's grip, several arrow shafts—each one sturdy enough to require considerable force to break—had shattered simultaneously.

Everyone froze.

Cesare let the fragments fall to the ground with casual indifference, as though he hadn't just demonstrated strength that could crush bone as easily as wood.

Through the veil of his golden lashes, Juan's eyes darted upward, gauging his brother's expression.

"Ah—I've overstepped again, haven't I, Brother? Forgive me. I know you dislike such... familial overtures. It's a flaw I cannot seem to correct."

"And what is wrong with wishing to cultivate family bonds?"

The Western Empress swept forward, the hem of her sapphire gown trailing across the grass like a banner.

"Though I suppose when one party is determined to remain cold-hearted, even ties of blood become meaningless. Such distance only impoverishes everyone involved."

Her words were ostensibly directed at Juan—a gentle reproach for trying too hard.

But everyone present understood the true target: Cesare, painted as the heartless eldest son who rejected his own family.

"Your Majesty."

The Empress glided to where the Emperor's staff had fallen and knelt gracefully to retrieve it. She pressed it into Perdiccas II's hand with an expression of devoted concern.

"Please, do not let this upset you. Such agitation is harmful to the heart."

The gesture was unmistakable—a display of loyalty to the Emperor above her own husband.

Just as she redirected the conversation back to safer ground—

"Your Majesty. Your Imperial Highness."

Cesare's languid voice cut through the carefully constructed moment like a blade.

All eyes turned to him.

He stood utterly at ease, the broken arrow shafts scattered at his feet, a faint smile playing at the corners of his mouth.

The smile of a man who had made his point—and was not yet finished making it.
