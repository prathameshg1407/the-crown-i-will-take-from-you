Chapter

The Venus
of Valdina

"Next time, I won't miss the neck."

"Get the girl!" the bandit leader roared, charging straight for Medea.

She didn't retreat.

Her fingers tightened around the fan. The iron handle was cool, steady—familiar.

In her past life, the Expedition warriors had laughed openly.

"Why did the Princess come? She can't even kill a low-level demon."

"She's dead weight."

Even Jason had turned his back on her. While she cried herself to sleep, he slept soundly beside her.

I can't live like this, she had realized then. If I want to survive, I have to protect myself.

So she watched.

She learned.

She trained in silence—studying leverage, angles, timing. How to let speed defeat strength.

The bandit leader was close enough now that she could smell him.

Now.

Medea stepped in.

The fan snapped shut.

Whack.

Steel ribs crushed into his larynx with surgical precision.

"—Ghk!"

He staggered, choking.

She pivoted smoothly, driving the weighted butt of the fan into his solar plexus.

Thud.

The air left his lungs in a wet gasp.

Before he could fold completely—

Crack.

A downward strike behind the ear.

Not lethal.

But absolute.

The man collapsed, twitching. A foul stench spread as his body betrayed him.

Silence.

The remaining bandits stared.

The "fragile" noblewoman had dismantled their leader in three movements.

Zip—zip—zip!

Knives screamed through the air.

"Your Highness!" Neril shouted.

Medea turned.

Two blades—

She swept the fan.

Clang. Clang.

The third was too fast.

Saya is behind me.
I can't dodge.

She braced.

Ping!

A flash of sparks.

Something small—fast—struck the knife midair, knocking it aside. It buried itself harmlessly in a tree.

Neril was already beside her.

"Are you hurt?"

"I'm fine."

Medea's eyes narrowed toward the forest.

Someone intervened.

"Who's there?" she murmured.

No answer.

Only shadows lengthening as the sun dipped below the trees.

"We're leaving," Medea said. "The gates will close soon."

Neril glanced at the unconscious leader, then back at Medea.

She hid this from me, Neril realized. She's not only a strategist… she's a fighter.

"Shall I finish them?" Neril asked coldly.

"No."

Medea wiped the fan clean.

"Let the cold take them. Or the wolves."

She looked at the trembling bandits.

"Tell your master this: Next time, I won't miss the neck."

They nodded frantically, dropping their weapons.

The Forest Edge

"Did you see that?" Gallo whispered. "The Princess can fight?"

Cesare stood half-hidden among the trees, eyes locked on the retreating carriage.

"Fan-based martial technique," Terence murmured. "Rare. Extremely controlled."

"And that last knife…" Gallo grinned. "Boss, that was you, right?"

Cesare didn't answer.

The image replayed in his mind.

Silver hair in fading light.

Calm amid bloodshed.

Not fear—command.

" The Venus of Valdina will save you. "

— The Shaman's voice echoed.

Venus.

The Morning Star.

Beauty—and war.

His heart thudded heavily in his chest.

Could it really be her?

"Gallo."

"Yes, Boss?"

"Find out everything about Princess Medea."

"Everything… like favorite food?"

"Everything," Cesare said quietly. "Her past. Her allies. Her enemies."

If she was the Venus—

I need to know whether she saves… or destroys.

The Princess's Palace

"From today on, you serve here," Medea told Saya. "Take care."

Saya bowed deeply.

The palace was a nest of sharks. The other maids eyed the street girl with thinly veiled disdain.

But Asilum Street had taught Saya worse lessons.

— Later —

"Any trouble?" Medea asked.

"There was resistance," Neril admitted. "But she's clever."

Down in the servants' quarters, Saya was already weaving her net.

"My birthday is the day my mother died," she said softly. "My father was crippled in the war… but he always bought me white bread."

Gasps.

"They said they'd sell my eyes," she whispered. "My organs."

Horror spread.

"If Her Highness hadn't saved me, I'd be dead. I know I'm lowly—but I'll repay her kindness. Even if it takes my life."

She bowed deeply.

"Please teach me. Scold me. I want to serve properly."

The maids melted.

Up on the balcony, Medea watched.

"She's good," she murmured. "She uses sympathy the way her brother uses rage."

"She'll be useful," Neril agreed.

"Yes," Medea said softly, eyes on the darkening horizon.
"Very useful."
