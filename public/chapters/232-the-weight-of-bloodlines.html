Chapter 232

The Weight of
Bloodlines

"Family binds tighter than chains—and cuts deeper than any blade."

Terence's voice was measured, careful—the tone of a man who had learned long ago which words could wound.

"Are you well?"

"As you can see."

Eratosthene's reply was clipped, offering nothing.

"I heard of your return. Yet you haven't gone to the mansion."

Terence spoke of the academy where he had once taught the young nobility, and of the Baron's estate in the capital—both still standing, still waiting.

The mansion, in particular, remained immaculately maintained. Every surface swept and polished, not a mote of dust permitted to settle. As though its keeper had never stopped believing its master would one day return.

And who ordered it kept that way...

"You already know my answer."

Eratosthene's expression remained as unyielding as carved stone.

"I don't yet understand why he left me in peace all these years. But I intend to settle matters now."

Perhaps this is for the best.

It would give him another opportunity to reach the young men who had turned deaf ears to his counsel—stubborn as walls, blind to the precipice before them.

"If that is your intention, then please—take the medicine."

Terence's voice grew urgent.

"The binding covenant has already exceeded its limits. Your body cannot endure much more."

Eratosthene suffered from a degenerative affliction—one that slowly petrified his lungs, turning each breath into a battle. For years, only careful medication had kept death at bay.

But after his daughter's death, he had refused every remedy offered to him.

"I have lived long enough. I hold no regrets about departing this world."

It was the quiet self-flagellation of a man drowning in grief—a father who believed he deserved to follow his child into the dark.

"You know he would not think so."

The words hung in the air like a blade.

Silence.

The atmosphere shifted—sharpened. Eratosthene's weathered presence, so often mistaken for harmless frailty, suddenly carried the weight of something ancient and dangerous.

Terence bit the inside of his cheek, tasting copper.

"Forgive me. I spoke carelessly—my nerves betrayed me."

"..."

"I ask for nothing grand. Only that you do not turn away from him."

"Even while bearing the Primal Curse, he kept your medicine safe. He never spoke of it, but he knew—his only true family was the late Empress... and you."

This was the first time Eratosthene had returned to the capital since abandoning politics decades ago.

Which meant that even as Cesare lay dying beneath the weight of the Curse, his grandfather had not come.

"I see."

Eratosthene's face remained as blank as a death mask, but something that might have been bitter amusement flickered at the corner of his withered mouth.

"Then perhaps he would prefer I simply died?"

The words were quiet. Almost gentle.

It would have been easier for him to hate me. He could have used my death as an excuse—a way to finally release the resentment he has nowhere else to direct.

The unspoken accusation found no voice, but it sank into Eratosthene's heart like a stone into still water.

"...My Lord!"

"Family."

The word dripped from Eratosthene's lips like venom.

"Such bonds are thick enough to set one's teeth on edge."

"..."

"Take care, young man. If you treat those who carry Katzen blood as though they were human, you may find yourself swinging from a rope—just as I nearly did."

Without waiting for a response, Eratosthene turned and walked away.

The cold hatred in his unhurried stride rendered Terence mute. He could not bring himself to call out again.

Step. Step. Step. Step.

The old man's footsteps dragged faintly against the marble, accompanied by the soft patter of smaller feet hurrying to keep pace.

Eratosthene became aware of the small presence at his side and paused, turning.

The little monk held out a handkerchief in his fern-delicate hand. With his other hand, he mimed wiping his own cheek—a silent question.

"Why do you offer that?"

[Because your eyes look sad.]

The boy raised his small hand, touched it beneath his eye, and drew it down his cheek. The universal gesture for tears.

Eratosthene went still.

The child could not hear, but he possessed an uncanny gift for perceiving what others tried to hide. And he never hesitated to name what he saw.

"Ah... perhaps I do."

His gnarled fingers moved of their own accord, gently brushing the skin beneath the boy's eyes.

Those eyes were so pure. So innocent.

Unlike another pair of eyes he remembered—golden as the sun, blazing with an ambition that sought to devour everything in its path.

" Grandfather! Take me with you, please? "

But not all predators are born with fangs bared.

The beast of prey had once been merely a fragile cub—small, lovable, desperate to be held.

Eratosthene had helped raise that child from infancy. He knew, better than anyone, the moment before those golden eyes had learned to contain their darkness. The innocent days before the abyss had claimed them.

"...Cough!"

As if to punish his foolish nostalgia, a violent cough tore through his chest.

"Cough! Cough!"

The spasms grew thicker, more brutal. The boy's eyes went wide with alarm, his small feet stamping in wordless distress.

When he tried to call for help, Eratosthene stopped him—one weathered hand on the child's thin shoulder, the other pressing a white handkerchief to his mouth.

When he lowered it, the cloth was no longer white.

It was soaked through with blood.

"Time... cough... there isn't much left..."

Eratosthene lifted his gaze.

Let this failing body hold together long enough. Just until I have atoned for my final sin.

Beyond the corridor's end, silhouetted against the dying sunset, rose the spire of the Emperor's Palace.

Valdina — El Amuz Strait

In the wake of cascading misfortune, House Claudio lay in ruins.

Raju had been executed. When Samon finally thought to investigate the secret assets his father had supposedly left behind, he discovered they had long since vanished—stolen, scattered, gone.

He could not have known that the wealth had already been recovered under Kensington's command and delivered directly into Medea's hands.

And when Samon learned that Raju's fortune had originally been pilfered from Emperor Katzen's own treasury, he lost all appetite for inquiry. The last thing he needed was to be branded an accomplice.

The situation deteriorated with terrifying speed.

"Treacherous Claudios! Get off Valdina's soil!"

Everywhere Samon went, scandal and contempt followed like shadows. He could scarcely step beyond his gates without weathering a storm of insults.

Even at the palace, crowds gathered at the walls to hurl abuse. The security forces, once so eager to curry favor, now turned their backs. The mansion fell into ever-deeper disarray.

"My lord... the servants, the gatekeepers—they're demanding higher wages. If we don't pay, they'll leave."

"Damn it all!"

Samon exhaled sharply, raking frustrated fingers through his disheveled hair.

Money. Everything comes down to money. And there's none left.

Yet he could not explode into rage as he once would have. There was no longer anyone to clean up after his tantrums.

His father was dead. His mother was useless. And Raju—the man who had truly held everything together—was gone.

That last loss cut deepest of all.

With Raju's execution, Samon's only remaining lifeline—his smuggling network—had begun to unravel.

"Apologies, my lord. We can't move any more goods for you."

"Our hands are tied! Stealing from Emperor Katzen himself? We're not looking to die!"

Word had spread that Raju's punishment stemmed from plundering the imperial treasury. One by one, the underground merchants severed their ties, fleeing like rats from a sinking ship.

"Still, my lord... shouldn't we search for Lady Birna? She's been missing for several days now."

The subordinate spoke with careful hesitation.

Though Birna did not share Claudio blood, she had lived her entire life as a daughter of this house. And through her mother, she was Samon's half-sister—bound to him by at least some thread of kinship.

"And who do you think is responsible for this catastrophe?"

A small voice whispered that this was wrong—that he should not abandon her. But Samon silenced it with a jerk of his chin.

"She's not my flesh and blood. Whatever time and coin I'd waste searching for her would be better spent finding a way out of this disaster!"

His subordinate stared at the floor, jaw tight.

So this is what "family" means to him.

In the end, it mattered nothing whether they were truly connected by blood.

What mattered was that Birna had failed to prove her usefulness—had, in fact, destroyed every remaining hope. And for that sin, she would never again be treated as a sister in Samon's eyes.
