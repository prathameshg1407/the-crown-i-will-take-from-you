"This wasn't chaos. This was choreography."

"There was nothing."

The words dropped into the hall like a stone into water.

"What?"

The court erupted.

Baroness Pinatelli

Baroness Pinatelli bowed deeply before the Queen Mother.

"I searched every room under your command, Your Majesty. The bedroom. The sitting chambers. The wardrobes. The jewelry box. There was no token in Princess Medea's possession."

"That is impossible!"

Madame Quiggin shrieked, the sound tearing out of her before she could stop herself.

She realized her mistake the moment the Queen Mother's gaze snapped toward her—but panic had already seized her tongue.

"Baroness Pinatelli, you must have overlooked something! Did you check beneath the mattress? Inside the lining of the jewelry box?"

Pinatelli turned slowly.

Her expression cooled to ice.

"Are you suggesting,"

she asked quietly,

"that I am incompetent?"

The room stilled.

"Or that I would lie to Her Majesty?"

"N-no! Of course not—"

Quiggin stammered.

"We searched with over a hundred attendants. We overturned furniture. We removed floor panels. If a rat had hidden there, we would have found it."

She loomed over the Head Maid.

"And yet, you speak as though you knew precisely where proof should be found."

Quiggin's face drained of color.

"And to discuss a Princess's private chambers with such familiarity,"

Pinatelli added coolly,

"is unbecoming of your rank. Your dignity seems… lacking."

The Queen Mother's fingers tightened on the throne.

Quiggin's mind reeled.

Why is she attacking me?
We were never enemies—

Her eyes flicked toward Medea.

The Princess was smiling faintly.

Princess Medea

"How strange. Head Maid, you sound very confident. Almost as if you were certain something was meant to be found."

Quiggin froze.

Pinatelli turned back to the Queen Mother.

"On my honor,"

she said clearly,

"I found no evidence in Princess Medea's possession."

A pause.

"However—"

The single word tightened every throat.

"Marieu,"

Pinatelli continued.

"Or should I call you Maria?"

Maria flinched as if struck.

"The token was found in your room."

"That's impossible! I never—!"

Pinatelli raised a sky-blue handkerchief.

Gold embroidery caught the light.

Minister Etienne

A ripple of revulsion spread through the hall.

The Minister was infamous—aged, grotesque, whispered about in every shadowed corridor. For a maid to possess his token was disgrace of the highest order.

"That isn't mine! Someone planted it!"

Pinatelli did not argue.

She simply unwrapped the cloth.

Inside lay a gold bracelet, delicate and unmistakable—

Trumpet vines curling around its links

Samon's bracelet.

"Several maids testified you've worn this recently. You claimed it was a keepsake from your mother."

"I—no—I—"

"So the bracelet is yours,"

Pinatelli said, voice sharpening.

"But the handkerchief wrapped around it is not?"

Silence crushed down.

"The trumpet pattern is restricted. Only the highest nobles may commission it. A maid's wages couldn't purchase a single link."

She stepped closer.

"If not the Minister… then who gave this to you?"

Maria's eyes darted wildly.

They landed on Duchess Catherine.

The Duchess was staring at the bracelet, her face tight, calculating.
