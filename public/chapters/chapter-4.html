Ambient: Dungeon
Chapter Continuation

Crack.

The sound tore through the dungeon,
sharp and sickening.

The maids flinched in the corridor, but none looked away. They watched, frozen, as a small, pale child methodically broke the spirit of the woman who had ruled the inner palace by fear.

A low groan escaped Madame Quiggin. Born a commoner and raised on soft silks and warm hearths, she had never known real violence.

She had never felt skin split open.

"Jena," Medea said.

Her voice, calm as a metronome, cut through the ragged breathing in the room.

"Y-yes?!"

Jena jumped, face drained of color.

"Why have you stopped? I told you to continue."

"I…"

Jena's eyes flicked between the sobbing Head Maid on the floor and the bloodied Neril hanging by the chains.

"Do not mind me," Medea said, raising the ash rod again.

"The punishment must not be relaxed. I would hate to interfere with a Head Maid's duties."

Jena's hand trembled. She struck—weak, halfhearted.

A slap. Then Medea's arm became a blur.

Whish—CRACK.

The ash rod bit into Quiggin's leg with surgical precision.

The rhythm of the room shifted. Jena's cruelty slowed and faltered; Medea's blows did not. For every pale, cowardly lash Jena landed on Neril, Medea answered with three clean strikes to Quiggin.

No sweat. No sighs.

She worked like a butcher trimming away rot.

"Ugh…"

Neril groaned; her head lolled forward.

"A-ah! Stop! Please!"

Quiggin screamed, clutching bleeding thighs.

She tried to crawl away; dignity forgotten, she became something small and human.

"Get up,"

Medea commanded.

Quiggin froze. The maids held their breath.

"I said, get up."

Medea's voice dropped like night. She looked down at the woman wrenched by pain, eyes flat and merciless.

"You are the Head Maid. Show some decorum."

"I… I can't…"

Quiggin sobbed. Her legs gave out; she crumpled to her knees, trembling.

The blaze of her pride had been doused. Survival had taken its place.

Quiggin gasped, forehead pressed to the cold stone.

"I… I yield,"

"I admit defeat."

"Please… forgive me."

Those words hung heavy and final.

The Head Maid of the Inner Palace knelt before the Forgotten Princess. By sunset the whole palace would know.

Humiliation tasted of copper and bile on Quiggin's tongue.

"Your Highness… I beg you…"

she whispered.

Medea watched a long, slow beat. Then her fingers relaxed.

Snap.

The ash rod broke clean in two, sounding like another gunshot.

The fragments clattered beside Quiggin's head.

"Jena," Medea said, turning her gaze to the torturer.

"Yes!"

Jena dropped the whip as if it burned her.

"Bring Neril. We are leaving."

Jena hurried to undo the chains. As she supported Neril's weight, Quiggin lifted her reddened face—eyes rimmed with blood, burning with impotent rage.

"You will—"

she hissed.

Medea paused at the door without looking back.

"Who knows," she murmured. "We shall see."

The iron door slammed.

The walk back to the princess's quarters was slow, the corridors empty and the shadows long.

"Leave us," Medea ordered as they reached her threshold.

Jena bowed so low her nose nearly brushed the floor, then fled into the dark, eager to escape the monster she had seen that day.

"Your Highness…"

Neril swayed, knees buckling.

"I've got you."

The imperious mask slid away. Medea caught Neril; the maid's weight made the girl stagger. Blood soaked into fabric, dust into skin.

Medea didn't care.

"Lean on me," she whispered. "Just a little further."

"The Head Maid…" Neril rasped. "She owns this palace… she'll come for you… I have to—"

"Shh. Don't speak."

A single tear cut a clean path through the dust on Medea's cheek.

In the life she'd left behind, Neril had died like this—alone, betrayed, struck down while protecting a mistress who'd turned too late.

Medea had believed the lie that Neril had abandoned her. She had been blind.

"I'm sorry,"

Medea choked.

"I'm so sorry."

"Your Highness? Why apologize?"

Neril blinked, confused.

"Because of me," Medea whispered, voice so low it was almost thought. "Because of me, you—"

She swallowed the word that would finish the sentence.

"Forgive me, Neril. For being blind."

"Why did you… Why didn't you stop them? You're precious. You shouldn't be—"

Her voice broke.

"Why did you stand there?" Medea asked, voice trembling now. "You are a knight. You could have snapped Jena. Why let them beat you?"

A bloody, weary smile creased Neril's lips.

"If I fought… they would have taken me away.

I couldn't leave you alone."

Medea buried her face in Neril's shoulder and wept like a child who had learned the cost of being foolish.

"This will never happen again,"

she vowed, fierce as a blade.

"I promise."

"Your Highness… please… don't cry," Neril whispered, heart breaking at the sight. "But promise—don't do that again. You could have been hurt. I can protect you better."

Medea managed a watery smile. "Looking at you now, I find that hard to believe."

"Your Highness…"

"Come. Let's get you cleaned."

Neril limped forward, supported by the girl who had sworn she'd die for her.

Medea did not voice it, but the thought burned with cold clarity: if they touched Neril again, she would not break a rod.

She would burn the palace to the ground.

Meanwhile

The Second Zone, Valdina's capital: a district of neat hedges and whispered grudges. There, among white stucco mansions and clipped roses, stood a house that was less home than bastion.

Its walls were alabaster, its gates thick. Rumor named its owner an arms dealer, a merchant of death with a private army. Locals crossed the street rather than meet his gaze.

On the third floor, sunlight spilled through a wide window.

"Boss! You have to hear this!"

Gallo crowed, waving a crumpled scrap of parchment. Tanned, scarred, and always on the verge of grin, he was a walking story.

"Fresh from the Palace! The Head Maid flogged—by the Princess. Can you believe it?"

He laughed, kicking his feet onto a cluttered desk.

"She marched to the dungeon, beat the torturer, and made the Head Maid kneel! The Head Maid surrendered first—ha!"

At the desk, a younger man studied a map and did not laugh.

Jet-black hair. Marble-carved features. Eyes like cold strategy—deep, detached, always measuring.

He traced a border line with a fingertip and let Gallo's news float over him without disturbing his focus.

"There is a princess in Valdina?"

he asked, voice flat, one eyebrow lifting as if it were a small, curious insect.
