Chapter X

The Defender
& The Defended

"To be understood by even one soul—is that not the rarest gift of all?"

Rather than returning to the banquet hall, Medea slipped through a side corridor that wound away from the festivities.

"Neril, inform His Highness that I shall meet him at the entrance."

The evening's objectives had been achieved. It was time to withdraw.

"Yes, Your Highness. Shall Lord Sissair accompany us?"

"Not tonight. Arrange for him to be taken to a secure location within the capital. In a few days, have him escorted quietly to the villa."

To preserve the carefully constructed narrative of this evening, it would be wise to maintain a visible distance from Sissair—at least for now. Any appearance of collusion would undermine the spontaneity of his dramatic intervention.

"Understood. I shall summon His Highness the First Prince."

As Neril departed, Medea continued down the long corridor with only Saya at her side.

She had taken perhaps a dozen steps when a figure emerged from the shadows ahead.

"...Marquis Eratosthene."

She recognized him instantly—the elderly man in austere gray robes, his bearing still proud despite the weight of years that bent his frame.

"Your Highness. The First Princess."

"I trust you will have a safe journey home this evening."

Medea offered a polite nod, already preparing to move past him. This was still the imperial palace, and from everything she had observed of the old Marquis, he was not one for extended conversation.

She was about to turn away when his voice stopped her cold.

"How is it that someone so clever cannot see what lies before her?"

The words emerged low and rough, like stones dragged across gravel.

"I'm afraid I don't understand your meaning, my lord."

"You orchestrate the Western Empress's downfall. You maneuver the entire court to place him upon the throne. Tell me—do you truly believe your sacrifices will be repaid?"

Medea lifted her gaze to meet the Marquis's.

He leaned heavily against his walking stick, his frail body trembling with the effort of remaining upright. Yet his eyes burned with an intensity that belied his physical weakness.

"Is it ignorance that blinds you to the cruelty of imperial blood? Or do you see the truth and simply choose to look away?"

A pause. His voice softened, taking on an almost pleading quality.

"This old man prays it is not the latter."

"My lord, I have always valued the kindness you have shown me. But I fear you overstep."

A flicker of displeasure crossed her features, quickly mastered. Her eyes, however, remained steady—filled with an unshakeable resolve.

Eleanor.

Looking at the proud princess before him, Eratosthene saw another face superimposed upon hers—a ghost that had haunted him for decades.

"Father, please don't worry. He loves me. We will never change. Trust me—trust my choice."

A bitter, almost imperceptible smile tugged at the corners of his weathered mouth.

He had watched from the shadows as this foreign princess methodically exploited every crack in Katzen's imperial edifice. He had recognized her brilliance from the start.

But tonight, watching her beside Cesare, he felt compelled to speak. To warn her before it was too late.

"No matter how brilliant your strategies, you cannot triumph against him—not with that naive faith that alliances will be honored."

His words were meant as armor for this clever woman—protection against the imperial family's honeyed venom.

"The moment he takes the throne, your sacrifices will be forgotten. Your very existence will become an inconvenience to be erased. How much of your life will you mortgage for a future that holds only betrayal?"

Perhaps it was because his words echoed accusations she had heard in another lifetime—poison hurled at her by voices she had trusted.

Medea paused.

"Marquis, you are a good man. But I suspect I understand the transience of political alliances at least as well as you do."

She felt no need to explain why she had chosen Cesare rather than Jason. That distinction was hers alone to make.

Yet she could comprehend the old man's bitterness. For years, he had watched his daughter raise an illegitimate child while enduring endless humiliations.

And in the end, he had lost that daughter forever.

Medea made to walk past him.

"It is my choice. I have made my own calculations. You need only wait and observe."

"Can you not see what lurks beneath that composed exterior?"

Her composure, her certainty—it reminded him too sharply of another stubborn woman who had once chosen a powerless prince against all reason.

"Cesare is capable of anything when survival demands it. As the one who has observed him longest... I can assure you of that."

Only his eyes seemed alive now—burning with accumulated hatred in a face as desiccated as dead branches.

Like a candle flame guttering toward its final breath.

"The blood of Katzen is cold and rapacious by nature. He will not rest content even after consuming both you and Valdina whole."

His voice cracked.

"His Majesty... was exactly the same."

"He will devour everyone around him. No virtue, no loyalty can conceal that truth—cough—"

A violent fit of coughing seized the old man.

His body swayed, colliding with a nearby chair. Something tumbled from the folds of his robes, rolling across the marble floor until it came to rest at Medea's feet.

A small wooden toy. Worn smooth by time and touch, half-rotted from years of secret keeping.

The moment her eyes fell upon it, something in Medea's expression shifted.

"You warn me not to be deceived by appearances."

She raised her head, meeting the old man's gaze directly.

"Yet how is it that a man so respected by the world cannot perceive—or chooses not to perceive—the simplest truth?"

Her voice remained measured, but fire crackled at the edge of every syllable.

"Was there anything he was permitted to choose? His birth? His station? The very nature you condemn him for?"

Medea thought of the ring that still rested in Cesare's private chambers—the Empress's ring, preserved like a relic.

And now this: a child's toy, carried close to an old man's heart despite everything.

A heartless man who cannot release the fragments of affection he still clings to.

She understood that impulse—the way certain memories refused to fade even when every relationship had soured beyond repair.

Why can this old man not see it? Why can the world not see?

"If you speak of rewards for loyalty, then I share your skepticism. I believe in neither promises nor alliances."

Heat rose unexpectedly in her chest. Yet her voice, when she spoke, was cold as mountain snow.

"What I believe in is a contract—an exchange where value given equals value received. And in that exchange, he has never once betrayed my trust."

"You, of all people, should know whether he is the kind of man who takes without giving—who swallows what he receives without acknowledgment."

"..."

"If you wish to deny even that, then we must agree to disagree."

Her gaze sharpened.

"But there is one thing we both know, is there not?"

Medea's eyes pierced the old man with an intensity that made him flinch.

"The origin of all these tragedies—the one you truly wish to condemn—is not Cesare."

She was unaccustomed to defending anyone with such passion.

And yet she wanted to.

No one else would. Not those who share his blood. Not those who have stood beside him for years. No one has ever offered him this—the simple gift of being believed in.

"If you will excuse me. I wish you a peaceful evening."

Medea felt no further sympathy for the ashen-faced old man. She departed without a backward glance.

And so she did not notice—

The quiet presence concealed behind a thin door, just a few paces away.

Medea's footsteps faded into silence.

"..."

Only then did Cesare allow himself to sag against the wall.

His elegant fingers—which had been wrapped around the door handle, poised to intervene—fell limply to his side.

It would be a lie to say his maternal grandfather's words left no mark—that accusation equating his nature with his father's did not wound.

But it was not unexpected.

No matter what he did, no matter how he tried to atone, he had long understood that the circumstances of his birth could never fully compensate for what Eratosthene and the Empress had lost.

What had truly concerned him was something else entirely.

Would the old man's accusations shake Medea's resolve?

Would she reconsider? Would she choose to leave?

That fear had driven him to follow when he saw them facing each other—a compulsion he did not fully understand.

"Was there anything he was permitted to choose? His birth? His station? The very nature you condemn him for?"

But Medea's voice—fierce, unwavering, defending him against Eratosthene's condemnation—had stopped him in his tracks.

Her words seeped into his chest like rain falling upon parched earth.

Joy and anguish twisted together within him—emotions so contradictory they defied separation.

Medea knew.

She understood that no one had ever truly stood beside him.

And yet she recognized the loneliness he had tried so desperately to ignore—had perhaps even hidden from himself.

Medea.

The one who had pulled him back from the precipice of death.

The one who understood him completely—perhaps more completely than he understood himself.

Even as gratitude swelled within him, threatening to overflow, Cesare was acutely aware of a darker truth:

He would be afraid of this feeling forever.

End Chapter X
