The Duke's Estate

Samon stared at the empty scroll.

The Royal Seal was gone.

"She asked for a bribe?" he said slowly. "Medea? The girl who thought money grew on trees?"

"Thirty chests of gold," Claudio muttered, pouring himself another drink. "She called it a fair price for a Minister's head."

Samon scoffed.

"Is she insane?"

"She's changed," Claudio said, rubbing his temples. "Quiggin drained her dry. Poverty does that—it teaches hunger. Now she wants to fill her own coffers."

Samon frowned.

Greed?

Medea had been naïve. Soft. Foolish.

But greedy?

It didn't sit right.

"Still," Claudio continued, waving a dismissive hand, "this is not entirely bad. If she moves for money, she can be bought. A greedy Princess is far easier to manage than a righteous one."

"The problem is the price," Samon said quietly. "We don't have thirty chests."

"I know!" Claudio snapped. "I can scrape together five. Maybe ten if I liquidate assets."

Samon's eyes sharpened.

"Then don't pay alone. Etienne didn't steal in isolation. His vassals, his merchants, his financiers—everyone profited. Make them bleed."

Claudio exhaled slowly.

"…Yes. I'll squeeze them."

He stormed out to send orders.

Samon remained behind, staring at the desk.

Thirty chests,

he thought.

Almost exactly the amount Etienne embezzled last year.

Coincidence?

Or a receipt?

The Princess's Palace

"Your Highness," Saya asked hesitantly, "are you really going to save that pig, Etienne?"

"If the Duke brings the gold?" Medea replied lightly. "No."

Saya blinked.

"Then why ask for it?"

"To buy time."

Medea turned toward the window, the Royal Prison visible in the distance.

"Thirty chests is not pocket change. My uncle must call in favors, liquidate holdings, threaten allies. He'll be busy for days."

She smiled faintly.

"And while he runs around begging… who visits Etienne?"

Saya's eyes widened.

"No one."

"Exactly."

"He already believes the Regent betrayed him once."

"Now he'll believe he's being abandoned."

"If it's out of sight," Saya murmured, "it's out of mind."

"And silence," Medea finished calmly, "breeds doubt."

She turned to Neril.

"Have Prime Minister Siseo visit the prison. Not to interrogate. Just to talk."

"Yes, Your Highness."

Medea exhaled softly.

"Now," she said, "bring in our guest. Umberto has been waiting."

The Interrogation Room

Umberto woke with a splitting headache.

Cold stone beneath his boots. Rope biting into his wrists. A hood over his head.

Damn it.

He listened carefully.

Heavy footsteps. Armored. Knights.

Then—

Silk.

A faint rustle, soft and deliberate.

Someone sat across from him.

The hood was ripped away.

Light stabbed into his eyes. He hissed, blinking rapidly.

Then he froze.

Silver hair.

Green eyes.

A porcelain face devoid of mercy.

The Princess.

Impossible.

The Red Foxes' assessment had been clear:

Queen Mother: irrelevant

Regent: dangerous

Princess Medea: dull, decorative, inconsequential

Not worth monitoring.

Yet here she was.

Calm.

Smiling.

Waiting.

"It's been a while, Umberto," Medea said, snapping her fan open.

His throat went dry.

"Y-Your Highness…?"

"Do you know who I am?" she asked gently.

"I—I am a humble servant. Everyone knows the Moon of Valdina."

"Stop."

Her voice dropped—soft, lethal.

"You are not a servant."

"And you are not humble."

She leaned forward, emerald eyes piercing.

"You are a Red Fox operative embedded in Etienne's household."

Umberto swallowed.

"And," Medea continued, "you burned a letter that would have saved your master."

Silence.

The room felt suddenly very small.

"I like competent people," Medea said pleasantly. "Especially ones who know when to disappear."

She closed her fan.

"I have a job for you."

Umberto stared at her, heart pounding.

The Fox had been caught by a spider.

And the web was already closed.
