Ambient: Court Intrigue
Chapter

The Serpent's
Game

"A gift is only a gift when it harms no one."

— Quiet Stratagems —

Medea sat with her gaze fixed beyond glass and stone, to where the banner of Valdina snapped and cracked in the winter wind.

“If we’re talking about the mentor… Marquis Gillipos lives in a small cottage outside the walls now.”

“Can you arrange a meeting?”

Her eyes never left the banner—green and gold, whipped fierce and straight by the wind.

“Without anyone knowing?”

Neril hesitated, then nodded.

“I’ll prepare it.”

Medea watched the banner as if it were a wound.

In the life she’d left behind, that flag had been torn down

by hands her family paid.

Not this time.

Knock.

The sound cracked through the quiet like a pebble on glass.

“Your Highness.”

A new maid slipped in, prim and nervous, holding her skirts as if they might tear.

“Duchess Claudio has arrived.”

The name tasted of ash.

Catherine Claudio swept in on a wave of perfume and silk so loud it nearly drowned the winter air.

She smiled the practiced smile of the well-fed and powerful.

A girl with loose pink hair—Birna—followed, too perfect and too young, the family’s pretty token.

“Your Highness!”

Catherine cooed, advancing with an embrace already rehearsed.

“I am so relieved you are awake. My poor darling—oh!”

She fussed, too fond by half. Birna flitted like spun sugar at her side.

Medea let their warmth pass over her like a draft. She had learned the texture of their affection:

sweet at the surface, rot underneath.

“You should not have come on such short notice,” Medea said softly.

“I was brought low with travel sickness,” Catherine purred, clutching Medea as if she might shatter. “Forgive me.”

“I brought a physician,” Catherine continued, bright and dangerous. “From Kazen—an imperial Divine Physician. He can mend even the crippled.”

The word Kazen tasted like a blade.

An imperial doctor in Valdina while Peleus bled on the border was not charity.

It was a message: our help comes with strings.

“And who is guarding the gates?” she asked, voice light. “Who checks the papers? Who verifies the credentials?”

Catherine laughed, a little too loud. “Mother spent a fortune. He is the greatest in his field. He will be at the gate presently.”

Birna’s eyes shone. “We’ll fetch him. He must see you first—Your Highness! You must be examined.”

Medea kept her face soft. She did not reach for the offense.

She used it.

“If he is so divine,” she said, widening her eyes with innocent charm, “would it not be best to present him to Grandmother first? The Dowager Queen has suffered with gout—she would be honored by such a miracle.”

Catherine blinked. “The Dowager?”

“Yes.” Medea’s small, polite smile sharpened into a blade only she felt.

“If the Queen, at her age and standing, were to be publicly visited by an imperial physician while the Princess was treated in secret, how will our people read it? Would they not say I hoarded foreign aid while the matriarch went without?”

The color drained from Catherine’s cheeks a degree at a time.

Her perfumed apology stuck in her throat.

Catherine opened her mouth and closed it again. “But—my dear, the doctor is meant for you—”

“And if he heals the Dowager first?” Medea pressed on, calm as frost. “The Dowager will be moved to gratitude that will be remembered in the court. The tongues of the capital will twist it into arrogance if we accept such favors for ourselves. They will say the Princess places herself above the crown.”

Catherine’s smile faltered. Birna’s grin thinned.

“You do not understand,” Catherine stammered. “He is a miracle—”

“If he is a miracle,” Medea said, “let him perform a miracle where it will silence the loudest enemies. My aunt’s pain is known to the whole court. Show him there. Let gratitude be recorded where it will matter.”

The room held its breath.

Catherine looked smaller suddenly—

silk no longer armor, perfume no longer a shield.

“Grandmother…?”

“Yes,” Medea said gently. “Take him to the Dowager. I insist.”

Catherine’s hand trembled on a fan she did not open. She had come prepared to present a coup disguised as kindness.

Medea had folded the gift and returned it to them.

“Of course,” Catherine managed at last, too smoothly. “If that is your wish…we will—of course.”

She bowed with a wounded grace and, for the first time since she entered, looked genuinely uncertain.

As they were escorted out—Birna clinging to her mother’s skirts, Catherine’s smile wobbling—

Medea let herself exhale once, quietly.

Neril watched her with narrowed eyes. “You turned a gift into a weapon.”

Medea’s smile was small, private. “A gift is only a gift when it harms no one. Now the Dowager will either refuse the doctor publicly—or welcome him on her terms. Either way, Catherine has been forced to choose in public.”

“And that will make her appear foolish or complicit,” Neril said.

“Both are useful,” Medea replied. “Emperor Kazen’s men at the Dowager’s bedside will be a spectacle—one that either humiliates my aunt or makes her look indebted to the Empire. Neither outcome strengthens the Regent.”

Neril nodded. “You leave no loose edges.”

Medea lifted her chin toward the banner.

“We cut the corruption out from the inside.”

Outside, the wind took the flag and flung it straight again.

Inside, Medea gathered the pieces of an alliance and set them where they would hurt the most.
